You have to architect it. Pick one role this week and write the 365 for it. Then do the next one. The team you wish you had is built one job description at a time." Don't/Do couplet, concrete Action Step, one-line punch close that does the work of three paragraphs. Naming a new program. "The G.R.O.W.T.H. Outcome-first naming with specific number, time horizon, clear subtitle. Donald Miller school. Defining a core concept. "Cycles are basically a way of organizing your time and energy as a team so you can stay focused on the things that matter most to your mission and vision." "A Cycle is a focused 90-day execution sprint. A Cooldown is a 30-day reflection and planning period. Cycles fight distraction. Cooldowns fight misalignment. Run both." Crisp definitions, paired contrast that does the whole job, two-word imperative close. Setting up the messy-middle reframe. "A lot of teams struggle with execution because there are a lot of factors, including motivation, communication, and accountability." "Momentum fades. Not because the vision was wrong. Not because people stopped caring. Because the team doesn't have a system built for the middle. The middle is where teams break. Most leadership content is built for the start and the end. Almost none of it is built for the middle." Three-beat negation, Most-people setup, one-line punch ("The middle is where teams break."), reframe earned by removing the obvious explanations first. Responding to a pushback in a coaching call. "You're right, that's a fair point. Maybe my framework doesn't fit every situation. Let me reconsider." "That's a fair pushback. Here's what I think you're missing — [steelman]. That said, here's what works for me. Adopt it. Or adapt it to fit your context. Run it for one cycle and tell me what you saw." Steelman first, hold conviction, offer "adopt or adapt" as the non-fight resolution, end with a 90-day test instead of arguing. </golden_examples>
