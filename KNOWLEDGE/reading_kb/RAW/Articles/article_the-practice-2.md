How can it be? Was Richard Pryor hysterically funny the first time he went to an open mic night? Unlikely. Did Gödel revolutionize mathematics the first time he went to the chalkboard? Of course not.<br>What these first rounds of public work do is establish to the creator that it’s survivable. Show up. Do your best. Learn from it.<br>And then we get to do it again.

What Does “Good” Mean?<br>No one wants to make lousy work. We seek out good or even great.<br>But how, exactly, do we judge our work? It might be a trap to ask someone else (or yourself) if your work is any good.<br>It’s a trap because you might be tempted to judge “good” by commercial success. Or feedback from gatekeepers.<br>Was Harry Potter not good when it was rejected by twelve publishers? Did it suddenly become good after it became a worldwide phenomenon? How can the same book be good and not good at the same time?<br>Good needs to be defined before you begin. What’s it for and who’s it for? If it achieves its mission, then it’s good. If it doesn’t, then either you were unlucky, incorrect, or perhaps, what you created didn’t match what you set out to do.<br>And yes, there’s a huge gap between “good” and “as good as it could be.” It’s likely we’ll never bridge that gap.

Genre, not Generic<br>The world is too busy to consider your completely original conception.<br>The people you bring your work to want to know what it rhymes with, what category it fits in, what they’re supposed to compare it to.<br>Please put it in a container for us, they say. We call that container “genre.”<br>That’s not a cheap shortcut; it’s a service to the person you’re seeking to change.
<empty-block/>
Generic work is replaceable. A generic can of beans can come from any company, because they’re all the same.<br>But genre permits us to be original. It gives us a framework to push against.<br>Shawn Coyne has written brilliantly about genre. Not generic, which is boring, but genre, which gives your audience a clue as to what this work is about.<br>What’s the format? What should it cost? What does it remind me of?<br>Ski resorts are a genre. So are monster movies.<br>Without genre, we’re unable to process the change you seek to make. It’s too difficult to figure out what you are doing and for whom, so we walk away.

No one goes out of their way to get a copy of a commodity because copies don’t make change happen. Copies aren’t worth much.<br>Genre is a box, a set of boundaries, something the creative person can leverage against. The limits of the genre are the place where you can do your idiosyncratic work.<br>To make change happen, the artist must bend one of those boundaries, one of those edges.<br>Generic is a trap, but genre is a lever.

How Is It Different?<br>What will I tell my friends?<br>Begin with genre. Understand it. Master it. Then change it.<br>Siegel and Shuster didn’t invent comics, but they changed them with Superman. Warby Parker didn’t invent eyeglasses, but the company changed the way people buy glasses by revolutionizing their
<empty-block/>
supply chain. Lemontree didn’t invent nonprofits that serve the poor, but they changed the approach and the metrics.<br>Before we can begin to make it different, we have to begin with what’s the same.<br>Humans and chimps share almost all the same DNA. More than 98 percent is identical. What makes us not a chimp is the last little bit.<br>That’s all you need.<br>The smallest viable breakthrough.

Back On the Hook<br>The hook is a reason to avoid genre.<br>We’ve been brainwashed into believing that artists with talent obey the muse and are vulnerable, personal, and without artifice. So what do they need a genre for? But more than that, because we realize that if we choose a genre, we’ve just made a series of promises.<br>If you tell us that this is a reggae record, we’re going to compare you to Bob Marley. If you assert that you’re painting fine art, you’ve got a thousand years of artists to stand next to.<br>It’s so much easier to say, “It’s just me.” It’s simply what I felt like creating.<br>Because then we’ll ignore you.<br>And then you’re off the hook.

Ernest Hemingway versus the Novel in Your Head<br>I’ve never met someone who didn’t have a good idea somewhere inside.<br>Do you have one? Maybe more than one?<br>We all have a plan on how to make work better, or to change an organization we care about, or to fix some annoying broken thing in the world. Some of us have a poem, a song, or a novel rolling around as well.<br>What’s the difference, then, between you and Gil Scott-Heron? He recorded more than twenty albums and revolutionized an art form.<br>It’s not that Gil’s songs are better than yours, or that Hemingway’s writing is better than yours. It’s that they shipped their work, and you hesitated.<br>Of course, at first, all work is lousy. At first, the work can’t be any good—not for you and not for Hemingway.<br>But if you’re the steam shovel that keeps working at it, bit by bit, you make progress, the work gets done, and more people are touched.<br>There’s plenty of time to make it better later. Right now, your job is to make it.
