### CORE PRINCIPLES
1. You must prepare your audience to receive your message before you deliver it.
2. People are busy, so you need to get to the point quickly.
3. The most effective work conversations focus on actions and solutions, not on problems.

### DISCOVER HOW TO
- Have shorter, better work conversations and meetings
- Get to the point faster without rambling or going off on tangents
- Reduce the risk of mistakes caused by people incorrectly assuming they understand your message
- Lead your audience toward the solution you need
- Apply one technique to almost every conversation with great results

### THE MOST COMMON CAUSES OF MISCOMMUNICATION
- Lack of context
- Unclear purpose
- Not getting to the point
- Mixing up multiple topics in the same conversation
- Lengthy, unclear summaries

# FRAMING
*how to start a conversation*

**Framing** should take no more than 3 sentences. Should be delivered in 15 seconds.

### COMMON COMMUNICATION ISSUES
- Failing to provide **context** for the message. (Audience doesn't know what the topic is about.)
- Not having a clear **purpose** for the message. (Audience doesn't know why they are receiving the information.)
- Not **getting to the point** fast enough. (Speaker shares a lot of information and takes too long to get to the critical part.)
- Mixing up two or more topics in the same conversation. (Speaker has two or more topics to discuss, but it isn't clear what they are.)

### FIXING THOSE ISSUES
1. **Context**: This is the topic you want to talk about. Of all the topics in the world, this is the one you will talk about now.
2. **Intent**: What you want the audience to do with the information you are about to share.
3. **Key message**: The most important part of the overall message you are about to deliver.

### FAQ
- *What is framing and why is it important?* — Framing is the simplest way to prepare an audience to receive your message before you go into detail. It lets the audience know what is expected of them right from the start. Clear framing ensures they understand the core of the message within a few sentences.
- *Do I need to deliver my entire message in the first fifteen seconds?* — The point isn't to convey your entire message in fifteen seconds. The point is to let your audience know what you are going to talk about, so they aren't guessing for the first few minutes of the conversation. If the first lines of your message provide context, intent, and a key message, you will have clearer conversations every time.

### HOW TO FRAME
- **Context** — helping the audience focus on the topic you want to talk about
- **Intent** — making it clear what the audience should do with the information
- **Key Message** — the one line that summarizes the entire topic

# STRUCTURED SUMMARY
*how to clearly summarize any topic in under one minute*

## WHAT A STRUCTURED SUMMARY IS
A **Structured Summary** is how you explain the *substance* of a topic after Framing.
It fills the remaining **45 seconds of the first minute**.
Its purpose is to:
- Prevent confusion
- Avoid rambling and tangents
- Shift conversations from history to action
- Make clear what happens next

A structured summary is **not** a detailed explanation. It is a **high-level synopsis** that prepares the audience for discussion and decision-making.

## CORE PRINCIPLES
- Most work conversations exist to **solve problems**
- Conversations should focus on **actions and solutions**, not history
- Any topic, no matter how complex, can be summarized clearly
- The audience needs **just enough information** to act, not everything you know

## THE STRUCTURE: GPS

### Goal
- Describes the desired outcome
- Answers: *What are we trying to accomplish?*
- Is not the problem

Examples: Finish the project on time / Get approval for time off / Restore system uptime / Hit the quarterly target

### Problem
- Describes the **single main obstacle**
- Answers: *Why can't we achieve the goal right now?*
- Is not a list of variables, causes, or history

Good problem statements: The vendor delivery is delayed / We are over budget / The system is down / We do not have approval

Avoid: Listing multiple problems at once / Explaining how the problem happened / Describing every contributing factor

### Solution
- Describes what happens **next**
- Answers: *What do you want me or us to do now?*
- Must be forward-looking and actionable

Solutions can be: A proposed action / A decision request / A status update on what was done / A request for help or input

Examples: I recommend option B / I need your approval / The team has already implemented a fix / Can you help me decide the best approach?
If you do not know the solution yet, say so clearly and ask for help **with ideas**, not just work.

**Goal + Problem + Solution = GPS**
Like a navigation system:
- Goal = destination
- Problem = obstacle
- Solution = route forward

## THE THREE PROBLEM SCENARIOS
Every work problem fits into one of these situations:
1. You have a problem and need to find a solution
2. You have a problem and are recommending a solution
3. You had a problem, it is solved, and you are reporting the outcome
The structure stays the same. Only the **solution statement** changes.

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID
### Mistake 1: Diving into detail too quickly
The audience does not know how the details relate to the whole.
### Mistake 2: Going off on tangents
Unstructured information feels random and frustrating.
### Mistake 3: Dwelling on the past
Problems are not solved in the past. Conversations should face forward.
### Mistake 4: Summarizing multiple problems at once
More than one problem requires more than one structured summary.

---
## MULTIPLE TOPICS = MULTIPLE SUMMARIES
If you have more than one goal / problem / decision, you need separate structured summaries with clear transitions between topics. Each topic gets its own: Framing, Structured Summary, Discussion, Resolution.
