- OUTLINING AS A FREEWRITER
- OUTLINE YOUR THREE ACTS
  Perhaps the least detailed version of outlining is to write down a simple sentence for each of your three acts
- Aristotle noted that every story has a beginning, a middle, and an end.
- Your first sentence will encompass the beginning.
  Your second sentence will cover the entire middle section. Your third sentence will cap everything off and end the story.
- CREATE A BEAT SHEET
- The next level of detail you can move to — if you’re comfortable with it — is a beat sheet, which is often used in screenwriting and Hollywood. Basically, a beat sheet is a list of all the major events in your novel.
    - Note: What’s the equivalent for non fiction
- If you’re going to go with a beat sheet, I’d suggest starting with these eight major events:
  inciting incident
  act one turn
  mid-point climax
  act two turn
  act two climax
  resolution
  denouement
  Give each of these one or two sentences — as much as a paragraph, if you must — and then you’ve got all you need to start tapping out your opus on your Freewrite.
  A beat sheet is a great middle ground. The minimal nature allows you to keep the story on track while giving you room to explore.
- 12 TIPS FOR DRAFTING FORWARD
- Remember that the goal of freewriting is to translate your thoughts into writing on the page
- Nothing else. Revising is a separate step in the writing process and should be reserved for later stages.
- If you haven’t tried draft- ing forward before — or maybe you have but you’ve gotten rusty — it’s helpful to follow several rules to avoid distractions and the editing-as- you-go that comes so naturally to writers.
- Save research for later (or start with it!). Yes, research is important for many forms of writing. But it can also quickly turn into a form of procrastination. Complete the bulk of your research before you start writing, or if it’s a topic you know well, commit to doing any fact-checking afterward. When you’re drafting and come to a place where you need to gather more information, simply leave a note to yourself right there in the text and continue drafting.
- Set a specific goal. Begin with a clear understanding of what you want to accomplish each time you sit down to write. This goal can be as tangible as an outline or notes written on sticky notes, or it can be simply, “Write for 25 minutes.” (If you enjoy timed sprints, check out the Pomodoro method. Find more information on page 91.)
- Fight your inner critic and commit to a messy first draft. Avoid the urge to critique or edit your work as you go along. Instead, concentrate on getting your thoughts down with- out judgment. We recommend stating it outright: “I am going to write a messy first draft.” Embrace that imperfection from the start so that you can write more freely.
- Eliminate external distractions
- Eliminating distractions before you get started is the best way to prevent time-consuming interruptions. We’ve done the hard work for you by creating Freewrite. Now, put your phone in the other room, turn off the TV, and start writing
- Turn off your inner spellcheck. Freewrite has no spellcheck or grammar check for a reason. Every squiggly line is a distraction, a moment that your writing flow is broken and you have to resist the powerful urge to go back
- to fix typos. Even if your eyes recognize typos, practice ignoring them until your brain is trained to do so automatically.
- Avoid overthinking. Trust your instincts and write without overanalyzing each sentence. Did that last sentence make sense? Who cares?! Anything goes in a messy first draft. Keep writing, and you’ll refine and revise later.
- Write quickly. This is just another way to trick your brain into writ- ing from that deep, creative place that can’t be reached when you’re overthinking. Strive for a flow state where you’re typing as quickly as your thoughts come to you.
- Keep moving forward. If you encounter writer’s block or a difficult section, resist the temptation to stop and dwell on it. Skip to another part and return to the challenging section later. We like adding a note to ourselves right there in the draft to remind us to come back to that spot when editing.
- Use placeholders. If you can’t think of the right word or need to look up a source, just insert a placeholder and keep writing. You will find those placeholders and fill in those gaps during the editing phase. (Read on for a more in-depth look at placeholders.)
- Accept redundancy. Often while drafting, a brilliant sentence will come to us. But it describes something we just described. What to do? Do not go back to delete and replace the first sentence. Simply keep writing the new one! These redundancies are easy to correct later.
- No backtracking. While Freewrite devices do have backspace keys, we’d like to challenge you to use them as little as possible. Or not at all. Remember, anything can be fixed later. But your writing flow is fragile.
- Experiment. Try different styles and approaches without judgment. You can compare and contrast and pick the best one later, during the editing stage. Right now, just focus on the joy of creating something out of nothing but thoughts in your head. Remember: This is a judgment-free zone.
- USING PLACEHOLDERS
- Placeholders are a great way to keep track of sections in your draft that you need to revisit later during the editing process. If you’ve never used placeholders, we suggest the following methods:
- X MARKS THE SPOT
- Every time you stumble while drafting, simply type “xx” and keep typing. You can include an explanation after the “xx” to tell Future You why you put the placeholder here. You can even close with another “xx” to mark the end of the explanation.
- When you finish drafting and transfer your work to editing soft- ware, you’ll use the search function to find all instances of “xx” and address each one individually.
- TAG, YOU’RE IT
- If you find yourself confused when you go back to view your “xx”s during the editing phase, or if you find that writing long-form notes to yourself interrupts your flow, then try using this standardized list of tags:
  [CHARACTER]: Insert this when you’ve forgotten some- thing about a character that needs to be included, or if you suspect somebody is acting out of character and may need to be revised.
  [DIALOGUE]: Use this when you reach a point of dialogue that’s going to need a lot of revision, or if an entire conversation needs to happen and it’s not coming to you at the moment. Drop this tag and move on.
  [PLOT]: This is your marker for when you think you’ve just introduced a plot hole. Stopping to try to unravel it now will interrupt your flow. It’s a problem for Future You.
  [SETTING]: Use this marker when you’re not positive the setting is consistent, or if you can’t think of an interesting setting to place a scene.
  [POV]: If you’re writing from multiple points of view, it can become confusing, especially when you’re freewriting. We’ve of- ten written entire chapters in the wrong point of view, only to fix it in revisions. If you suspect you may have gotten the POV wrong, insert [POV] and keep writing in whichever POV feels natural and keeps you in flow. (This may even be a hint from your subconscious that you should change the POV!)

- THE PSYCHOLOGY OF WRITER’S BLOCK
- As a book coach and former psychologist, I’ve spent more time than most thinking about the psychology of writer’s block. There are five common types I see in my clients:
  Feeling creative but not being motivated
  Feeling creative but too distracted to write
  Being stalled by self-doubt or imposter syndrome
  Having an empty well where you just can’t get any ideas flow- ing
  Mental exhaustion that prevents even mundane tasks from getting done
- you’re unsure which type of writer’s block is holding you back, complete this sentence (quickly, without thinking): “I can’t write be- cause…”
- You’ll be surprised how honest your mind is. Knowing is half the battle, and if you constantly have the same response to this question, that will teach you a lot about the mental blocks you need to process. Unfortunately, there is no single magic trick to banishing writer’s block, and finding what works for you requires experimentation. The four techniques I use with my clients are mindset, routines, writing sprints, and the zero draft model.
- MINDSET
- You started the mindset portion by completing the “I can’t write be- cause” statement. If you ignore something, it stays in the back of your mind. So don’t ignore it! Instead, acknowledge this negative thought. But being willing to sit with a negative thought goes against everything the self-help world tells you about viewing the world through rose-colored glasses, right? Now, I’m not saying you will dwell in this negative place. No, you’ll say hello and promptly walk away after completing the following sentences:
  “I want to write because…”
  “I will write because…”
  These statements together will let you address the block and provide internal motivation to write.
- The answer to “I can’t write because…” is often related to impost- er syndrome, fear, or comparison
- ROUTINES
- If you look up any prolific writer, you’ll learn about the power of writing routines. The mind makes 2,000 decisions per hour. Habits take the pressure off by allowing some of those choices to be made on autopilot; use those “saved” decisions to set yourself up to write.
  Additionally, habits train your brain to get into writing mode by associating a stimulus with the desired activity. For example, I outline at my desk, draft at the kitchen table (or couch) on my Freewrite Traveler, and edit at my computer
- in Scrivener. I also do all my heavy creative work in the morning and editing in the afternoon. So when I’m at the table with my Freewrite, my mind instantly associates that location and tool with writing. It takes significantly less time to get into a flow state.
- SPRINTS
- Every psychologist knows we all respond to a ticking clock differently, so I want you to approach sprints in a way that works for you.
  The first — and most common — way to use sprints is as a personal challenge: set a target word or page count goal to hit during a certain amount of time.
  The second option is viewing it like an appointment and making that time sacred in your schedule without the added pressure of hitting a word count.
  A third option that I love is hosting a coworking sprinting session in person or online. These group writing adventures provide accountability, foster friendships, and can lead to the development of critique groups or writing buddies to toss ideas back and forth with.
- If you don’t write well under pressure or seeing your word count distracts you, opt to leave the display blank or use an analog timer to signal the end of your sprint.
- THE ZERO DRAFT (AKA THE MESSY FIRST DRAFT)
- All of this has been building up to writing a zero draft. You might have heard this called a messy first draft, a vomit draft, fast drafting, or forward drafting. Since I’ve worked in the startup world, I prefer “zero draft”; it’s much like an MVP, or minimal viable product. An MVP is certainly not perfect, but it’s enough to test and build on an idea.
- I always tell my clients that drafting is for getting your ideas out, and editing is where you shape your words into a beautiful story
- Allison Williams perfectly summarizes the revision process in her book, Seven Drafts: Self-Edit Like a Pro from Blank Page to Book. While the thought of seven drafts might make you cringe, it’s pretty close to reality for many writers. You improve with each book you write, mean- ing you might combine some of the seven drafts when you’re further along in your writing journey. But what holds true is you’ll never reach perfection on a first or second draft

- THE PROCESS
- Here is the workflow I use to write on my Freewrite:
  I mindmap my ideas on paper or create an outline before I tackle larger projects.
  I do my freewriting early in the morning before I get online and freewrite in the afternoon as a break if I’m feeling over- stimulated.
  I create new Freewrite documents for each blog post, email, podcast/video script, or several social posts.
  I use Markdown to denote subheadings and break up my longer posts. I also leave notes for myself with **’s, to remind myself to do something during revisions — like move a section to the introduction, for example.
  After I’ve written a draft, it automatically syncs, via Postbox, to my Google Drive.
  I often wait a day between writing on my Freewrite and edit- ing my drafts to have fresh eyes. I open my drafts in Google Docs and make
- quick grammar and spelling fixes.
  If it’s a newsletter or social post, I’ll make other edits directly in Google Docs. I might rewrite awkward sentences, change the order of things, or delete sections to make the writing solid.
  If it’s a larger project, I’ll copy and paste the content into Scrivener for easier editing. I use ProWritingAid to do a more thorough edit on longer projects.
  Then I hit publish, either through my newsletter, a social media scheduler tool, or exporting from Scrivener.
