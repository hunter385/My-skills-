> Humans consistently make decisions that contradict what we *say* we want — health, financial stability, good relationships. This isn't because we're malicious or stupid, but because our minds are wired for **self-deception**. The core mechanism is **confirmation bias**: the built-in tendency to search for and privilege evidence that supports what we already want or believe. Instead of truth quests, we run **confirmation quests**, filtering out disconfirming input and inflating anything that validates our current inclinations.
>
> Jeremiah's insight — *"the heart is deceitful"* — explains why the problem is deeper than dishonesty. Dishonesty is obvious; **deceitfulness mixes truth, half-truths, and lies** so smoothly that we trick ourselves. This inner salesman persuades us into choices we later regret, even ones we previously warned others not to make. Smart people are especially vulnerable because self-deception feels like rationality in the moment.
>
> There's **no cure**, but there is a practice: ongoing supervision of our inner narrative. Three disciplines help:
> 1. **Admit it:** Accept that your heart will deceive you. This humility creates the suspicion necessary to notice when internal sales pitches begin.
> 2. **Ask it:** "Am I being honest with myself… really?" Using your own name disrupts autopilot and forces genuine reflection. Even if you don't plan to act on the truth, you owe yourself awareness of it.
> 3. **Be curious:** Curiosity (Brené Brown's "emotional curiosity") punctures the protective stories we tell ourselves. When discomfort arises, stay with it instead of retreating; it reveals the motives beneath our impulses.
>
> Self-deception is permanent, but so is the possibility of catching it. The rare person who seeks disconfirming evidence, welcomes contradictory input, and questions their own motives becomes both wiser and safer — less vulnerable to their own heart's unexamined stories.
>
> Am I being honest with myself . . . really?
> Am I telling myself the truth or selling myself a regret?
