# Manager Time
---
managers divide their time into the smallest chunks possible.
they have twenty chunks in a day from 20 minutes to 5 minutes
for managers, an empty time slot is a missed opportunity
it's time that did not get a return on investment for them.
They talk to many different people. They do lots of different tasks all day long
they begin the day prepping for their meetings.
they end their day whenever the last meeting is finished.
a fully booked day is a fully maximized manager… because, they're getting a return for all the moments of their day.

# Maker Time
---
creators. entrepreneurs. people that make big things
this is the deep work
this is the stuff that moves the ball forward
this is quadrant 2 — not urgent but very important
these are the things that when you look back at the things that moved the ball forward for your business, these are the slots when that happened.
these are the times when you create something that did not exist before you started.
Maker time thrives on only a few large chunks of uninterrupted time
you only have one or two per day
if you had two per day, that means you only have 10 chunks per week.
do you really want someone to steal one of your 10 blocks?

| Aspect | Manager Time | Maker Time |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Focus | Meetings, coordination, task switching | Deep work, creation, building something new |
| Time Structure | Many small chunks (5–20 minutes) | Few large uninterrupted chunks (1–2 per day) |
| Daily Rhythm | Starts with prep for meetings, ends when last meeting is done | Anchored around protecting deep focus periods |
| Measurement of Success | A fully booked calendar = maximized productivity | Protecting and using deep work blocks = real progress |
| Opportunities | Empty time slots feel like missed opportunities | Empty space is necessary for focus and creativity |
| Output | Conversations, decisions, coordination | New ideas, products, strategies, and breakthroughs |
| Pace | Fast, varied, high interaction | Slow, deliberate, focused |
| Risk of Interruption | Low impact—easy to slot in another small task or meeting | High impact—losing one block means losing up to 10% of weekly deep work |

# When The Manager Helicopters The Maker
*The maker / manager conflict*
---
When managers don't understand how makers work, they destroy the productivity of their team.
managers often assume that makers can work like they do… on demand. easily interuptable.
manager: "hey, it's just 30 minutes. you got free time. In fact, i looked at your calendar, you don't have anything on there today anyways. you're barely even working…"
they're using their measuring stick, their way of working, and transposing that to the maker.
> The manager accomplishes work through meetings, while the maker is productive when they're not in meetings.
when the manager tries to make the maker be more like them, they both lose.

# How To Help The Makers + Managers on Your Team
---
**MANAGERS**
- help the makers on your team have deep blocks
- batch the meetings

**MAKERS**
- you have to have meetings. it's part of the job.
- you need the managers to help you complete your work
- if there is a meeting that has to happen, make sure you fill up the rest of that morning with any other meeting you can think of to get them all done in one morning. batch them. don't refuse them.
