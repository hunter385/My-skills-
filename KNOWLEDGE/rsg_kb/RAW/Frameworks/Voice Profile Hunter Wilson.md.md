## SECTION 1: BELIEFS & CONTRARIAN TAKES

### Q1: What's something you believe about church growth or pastoral leadership that, if you said it out loud at a typical conference or in a typical pastor mastermind, would get you side-eyed, dismissed, or quietly written off?

The business world has a lot to teach the church world how to become better at church and leading an organization.

### Q2: Name the specific business idea, practice, or thinker that you've quietly imported into Ready Set Grow that most pastors in your world would either reject, get suspicious of, or call "too corporate" if they knew you were using it.

The business thinkers that I've incorporated into Ready Set Grow are: David Allen, Tiago Forte, Nick Milo, Gina Wickman, Josh Kaufman, James Clear, Alex Hermozi, Russell Brunson, Dan Sullivan and Benjamin Hardy, Brendan Burchard, Jake Knapp and John Zarecki, Donald Miller, Michael Hyatt, Greg McKeown, Ryder Caroll, Jason Freed and David Heinemeier-Hansson.

  

The thing that I don't know how to make it controversial feeling, but most of the things that I'm taking from this list of people is personal organization and high-performance habits: how do you build systems for your own life to be organized and highly productive? How do you help a team be highly organized and productive? Be good at management. Most pastors suck at managing their teams. They're very disorganized visionaries.

### Q3: Finish this sentence: "The reason your team isn't executing isn't because they're lazy or unspiritual. It's because you ___________."

Aren't good at keeping the team focused on one thing. You have too many ideas and whiplash in them everywhere, whenever you have a new idea. You change directions too much.

### Q4: Most pastors would tell you their vision is their gift. What's your comeback to the pastor who thinks his constant new ideas are the leadership?

I think your ideas are amazing, and the things that you hear from God are amazing. It's the timing of when you try to implement them that is causing your team to have resistance. If you started operating on cycles and cool downs and bring up your ideas inside of the cool down instead of whiplashing your team inside of a cycle, that's when your team is going to love being a part of an innovative team. Everybody wants to be on an innovative team. Most people just don't like the timing of the implementation of the idea. When you have the idea, instead of dumping it onto a person because you can't keep track of your own stuff, why don't you write it down on a shared ideas list that we'll bring up in the cool down?

### Q5: What's another belief about pastors, churches, or growth that you think is just true, but most church consultants would either disagree with or be too afraid to say publicly?

Almost all of the evangelical churches today are so boring and vanilla, and they're all trying to do the same things. Everybody is just this boring, vanilla bland of the same thing.

### Q6: What are the specific things every evangelical church is doing that's making them indistinguishable from each other?

When I think of the vanilla playbook:

  

Everybody's doing the same worship songs. The pastor has a shallow sermon that's disorganized, and you can tell that he wrote just a couple of days ago. It's going too long, it's disorganized, and there are no frameworks that I can actually utilize. Every church is trying to get their people to go to a growth track and become a part of the army that is serving with them just to keep the systems running. After you get saved and baptized, it's kind of like good luck finding it out on your own. I wish there was a way that you could have almost like John Mark Comer's Rule of Life type of concept, where you're teaching people frameworks of: here's the life operating rhythm that Jesus teaches, and here's how we're going to enhance your life plan according to the life operating rhythms that we run on together as a church that works with your rule of life.

  

No one teaches frameworks and is teaching concepts that fit inside of a framework. It's just new teachings every single week that no one is actually implementing, because it can't keep up with the pace of just another sermon, another sermon, another sermon with more action steps, with 15 points inside of the sermon with 12 action steps. It's just boring and it's vanilla. No one is paying attention. Everybody is just coming to the church out of obligation. It's really on your own to try to figure out how to grow. They're optimizing for the top of the funnel, the top new people getting them saved, and then they don't know what to do to help increase the depth of people that are following Jesus already.

### Q7: What does the average pastor's sermon prep process actually look like, in your honest assessment? And what should it look like?

Most pastors try to follow some kind of 30,000-foot framework inside of their teachings. That's the first basis for making it sound cookie-cutter, because they're actually using a cookie-cutter to write their sermons. They're following a master framework that Andy Stanley or another person that they follow created, and they're not really filling it in with creativity. They are doing basic platitudes as points, like "pray more" or "Jesus loves you," type of shallow, non-memorable, multi-step processes, because they don't really have one sticky point that they're really trying to nail home of how it fits inside of a larger macro framework. They're just teaching three points here, four points there, and a lot of it is just cotton candy fluff that is just trying to get through the 30 minutes because they didn't have time to be brief.

  

I think what should happen instead is pastors should be more dedicated to preaching and delegate more things so that they have more time. Time to get multi-weeks ahead, and they need to have some kind of reverse engineering of a macro framework, not teaching from scratch on a new topic over and over and over again. It's: How does this piece of what I'm teaching fit into the macro framework of my life plan that I'm teaching people, like a rule of life? What is the macro framework that we use to run our lives and live our lives? How is what I'm teaching inside of this series fitting into the bigger picture here?

### Q8: Which big-name pastors, teachers, or church voices do you think are actually overrated as strategic operators?

I have no problem with Andy Stanley. I think he's incredibly creative and an incredible communicator. I just think the problem is when people follow a framework but they're actually not learning themselves about new concepts. They're not having a personal reading and learning journey. They're just regurgitating things that they learned a long time ago and doing their points of the sermon as platitudes that don't really have any substance to them.

  

I think the real issue is not the actual teachers like Andy Stanley or Greg Surratt or Kerry Newhoff. It's actually that the pastors don't really have any substance to them because they're so busy playing whack-a-mole and putting out fires. They don't even have any kind of depth or new concepts that they're learning about and deepening to themselves. What they teach is pretty empty and shallow themselves.

### Q9: What's your honest take on salvations and baptisms as the scorecard?

I don't really have a problem with salvations and baptisms. I think that's cool that more people are getting into heaven. I think what I have a problem with is that most churches are optimizing for Sunday attendance and trying to get perfect attendance and trying to get more from their people. They have a getting mindset instead of a giving mindset. What can I get from people? Not what can I give to people? I feel as though most churches really ultimately care about clever or creative or, at the worst, manipulative ways to get more from people instead of equipping them for their life. They're equipping them to be more involved in the church.

### Q10: In the world of team building, leadership, and personal productivity — what's a piece of conventional wisdom you think is just wrong? (Reframed away from "church" since Hunter writes broader than that.)

I agree more with the contrary takes of 37 Signals, where I think five-year plans and five-year visions are totally impossible to imagine, especially in an entrepreneurial type of business where they have a lot of ideas. There's no way you can imagine what your church or your organization is going to look like in five years, because we're terrible at estimating how long things take. I don't even like doing annual planning. I like annual goals for the year, but I like approaching it cycle by cycle. I like 90 days for a church and six-week cycles for a business, to where we are only planning one cycle ahead and we're only thinking one cycle at a time. I don't believe in long-term vision. I think it's impossible to estimate.

### Q11: When a pastor says, "I need a 5-year vision. Scripture says vision matters. Are you telling me to throw all that out?" — what's your exact response?

I really like the idea of a directional statement more than an actual mission statement. Basically, this is the direction that I'm heading, and as long as I'm directionally correct, that's all I really care about.

  

I believe that goals and vision, especially in the long term, are written in pencil. They're not written in ink. They can be adjusted along the way as you start to taste more things and filter more things and understand the "walk a mile, see a mile" type of concept: we're learning and adjusting as we go. We don't grip tight to anything that we don't fully yet understand, because my long-term vision is the furthest away that I'll ever be from that long-term vision and I know the least about it when I make the plans today. Why would I give up the idea that I'm going to get smarter as I go? I want the smarter me to make the adjustments along the way as I start to realize and have more of an understanding of what my life and my organization are supposed to look like. I want to reserve the right for myself to get smarter over time and make adjustments along the way.

### Q12: What's your take on "Hire A-players. Only A-players"? And what's the version of team building you actually believe in for the small-to-medium organization?

I think everybody ideally would love to have A players, but there's this thing called a budget that kind of prevents all of us from doing so. There are a couple of things, too:

  

1. If you're going to hire A players, you first have to be an A player or an A-plus player, because no one wants to work for you if you're a B leader.
    
2. You have to have an A-plus vision. You have to have an A player that A players are attracted to, with A-player compensation, so they feel even excited to join your team.
    

  

I think what's way more realistic is:

  

1. You need to become an A player first before A players are attracted to you.
    
2. Yes, have an A player or maybe two for your team, but what's more realistic is to hire puppies, which can turn into stars over time. Puppies fall in love with your culture first and need training to develop into a star. You hire them when they have potential and you train them up to become stars over time.
    

  

It's really more of a mix of A players mixed with puppies, I think, that is more realistic, and you train up the puppies by partnering them with a star.

### Q13: What's the conventional wisdom about content creation, personal brand, or building an audience that you think is straight-up wrong?

I think the message that a lot of people are hearing is that you have to go viral or you have to have a big audience to make a lot of money. I think instead, a small audience can make a lot of money if you sell your own coaching programs that are high-ticket. The content that you make is really good and it builds the know, like, and trust of the teacher without you actually having to trade your time for money.

  

I think the content needs to be quality, and you need to give away your best stuff and not hide it behind the paywall, because Alex Hormozi says, "If you only put out your B content, people think you're a B leader." You have to believe that people will want to pay for the implementation even if you give away the information, because people can't self-implement.

### Q14: What's a belief you hold about your own customers (the pastors who pay you) that they'd push back on if you said it bluntly to their face?

I definitely think that pastors are addicted to the high of a new idea. Because they have the luxury of delegating that idea, they're not a part of the execution of the ideas they come up with. When you delegate the ideas, you don't have to be a part of the execution. You have more free time to come up with a new idea, and you're never actually implementing any of the ideas that you actually want to see happen. You just want the dopamine hit of people validating your idea, when in reality, are they validating your idea because it's actually good or because you're their boss?

### Q15: What's the dirty secret of the mastermind industry — and what's the line you've drawn for Ready Set Grow that's different?

In the church industry, most people that are teaching these masterminds have a full-time job of still being a pastor, and so they teach one-to-many conference-style talks that are somewhat tactical and inspiring, but people can't actually pull them off. People can't self-implement. Our people are actually getting results because of a couple of things:

  

1. This is the full-time thing of what we do.
    
2. We're really, really good at teaching frameworks that are teaching them how to think, not just giving them tactics and forcing them to come back to us, the masterminds.
    
3. They need really, really often accountability on what they said they were going to do, because they get distracted, they have new ideas, and they have their own church that they're running on top of all of the ideas and innovation that we're teaching them.
    

  

I really think the teaching frameworks, not just teaching points in a conference talk, is a massive difference. Another thing is this isn't just special green room access that the lead pastors get or the CEOs get. It's teaching their whole team, so they're not having to play the telephone game with their team once they get back and say, "Hey, we have to change everything. I just went to this new conference, and here's what we need to do now." That's why it doesn't work in most other coaching programs. They give them a lot of information and gold, and they have to go back and play the telephone game, and they can't really teach it the same way they were taught in the room. I think another key component of this is teaching the frameworks, but also having the growth plans that we teach and lead people through every cooldown. That helps them to understand: out of all the things we could be doing, here are the right things to focus on. We're teaching them how to prioritize the right things so that these lead pastors don't get their teams off focusing on things that are stupid and not really producing any results. I think another thing about our coaching calls is we try to do workouts as an approach to teaching on the Zoom calls instead of monologues. We try to give them time to actually implement the things that they're learning on the calls and not expect them to take it all as homework that they'll do later, because they probably won't do it as homework later. By the time they actually leave our coaching sessions, they actually have implemented something that they can now put to use, because they did a live workout on the call. It's kind of like Peloton workouts, but for their productivity and leadership.

## SECTION 2: WRITING MECHANICS

### Q16: Walk me through how you actually write. Not the ideal version. The real one.

So I usually have multiple screens open, and I have Obsidian Excalidraw out so I can draw frameworks, and I have AI with me as well. Most of the things I'm teaching now I have already drafted in the past. It's like I'm teaching my macro frameworks, and I'm pulling out something that I need to deep dive on for this particular lesson. So I teach AI by pulling in my old drafts if I haven't already, so it learns the frameworks of what I'm trying to teach. Ultimately, I'm really bad at teaching from a manuscript where I bullet out word for word every single word of what I'm going to say and memorizing it. I don't have time to memorize the talks that I'm doing, and I don't like doing it. It puts me in more of a presenter. I'm presenting a talk rather than teaching them something that they can utilize.

  

What I really like doing is having some kind of framework that I can draw on my Remarkable or a whiteboard. Remarkable, if it's a Zoom call, and a whiteboard if it's something live. I like drawing out a framework that I can teach from, and I like drawing or showing pictures or metaphors for what I'm trying to illustrate. I feel like I teach at my best when I'm teaching from a picture or I'm presenting slides that show visuals and frameworks that actually help me to be in a more improv, flow, natural voice type of presenting mode. I'm illustrating the concepts for them to take, and I'm teaching them how to think through frameworks that they can have themselves. So I'm asking AI to help me to come up with frameworks and come up with visuals and come up with ideas of illustrations of how I can come to life with either a slide deck in Apple Keynote or drawings that I can do inside of my Remarkable to illustrate what I'm trying to get them to understand. A lot of the times, people ask for my drawings or frameworks afterward so they can keep them and utilize them themselves.

### Q18: What's the gut-check for a good hook vs. a bad one?

I really like to teach things that I've experienced or felt myself. If it feels authentic to me, I'm like, "Man, that's what I would have wanted to hear." That's when I really feel like it's something that I want to present to other people. Or if I've helped one of my clients through a specific problem that maybe isn't a problem for me, but I've helped somebody that has had that problem find a breakthrough, that's when it feels like, "Okay, I'm going to go with that."

Usually, what I do is do one or two slides, or one or two visuals, or one or two "Have you ever felt this type of problem?" slides before I introduce "Here's the solution." ### Q34: Emphasis — how do you make a key idea land harder?

I would say I bold. I sometimes put it in all caps. I sometimes put it in a call out box to where it is really emphasized in my writing.

  

In speaking, I slow down. I definitely probably repeat it twice, and I probably lower my voice.

  

I think you make a key idea feel like a key idea by making it the thesis of your talk and reverse engineering everything around that main point. You don't have too many main points, and if you do have steps in a process, they're not the main point. There are steps to pull off the main point or to make the main point happen. I think every talk should be about one big thing. In all of the steps, reverse engineer those steps to get to that outcome.

  

If you have too many main points, nothing lands anymore. If you have too many points that they each separately have to pull off or have to remember, then there's nothing they're actually going to remember.

### Q35: Stories — when you use one, what kind do you reach for?

- a client breakthrough story
    
- stories from my own past
    
- stories from my kids
    
- things that I've actually gone through, usually than telling somebody else's story
    

  

I don't ever tell stories of history or other people. I would usually only tell personal stories.
