## The reframe

Most pastors have a calendar full of reactive work. They show up to whatever shows up. By Friday they're exhausted, but the Cycle Rocks haven't moved.

The Ideal Week is a calendar architecture, not a schedule. It pre-decides what kind of time each block of the week is for — before anything has been scheduled. Then schedule items get fitted into the architecture, not the other way around.

## The framework

The Ideal Week is built in four moves:

1. **Identify the time-categories you need to protect.** For Hunter, these include: Deep work / framework creation. Filming days. Coaching calls. Reading / capture. Family. Replenishment (running, etc.).

2. **Pre-allocate blocks of the week to each category.** Mondays AM = deep work. Thursdays = filming. Etc. The categories live on the calendar before any meeting gets scheduled.

3. **Schedule reactive work inside the architecture.** A meeting request goes into a meeting block, not a deep-work block. A creative ask goes into a creative block. The architecture protects the priorities.

4. **Review weekly: did the architecture hold?** This is where [[Ideal-Week-Accountability]] comes in — checking whether the actual week matched the ideal one.

## How to use it

- Build the Ideal Week during a Cooldown, alongside the next Cycle's Rocks.
- Time-block the calendar in 2–4 hour chunks, not 30-minute slots. The architecture is about kinds of work, not granular tasks.
- Use [[Weekly-3-Daily-3]] inside the blocks. The blocks say "what kind of work." Daily 3 says "what specifically."
- Treat the Ideal Week as a hypothesis, not a contract. Each week, calibrate.

## Why most calendars fail

A typical pastor's calendar is built additively: meetings, asks, appointments accumulate, and "deep work" tries to find space in the gaps. There are no gaps. The work that compounds is the work that gets a protected block.

The Ideal Week inverts the order: protected blocks first, accumulating asks second. The calendar becomes a Cycle Rock-protection device, not a scheduling backlog.
