## The reframe

Short-form is not "long-form, shorter." It's a different structure entirely. Most creators improvise. They open with a relational warmup ("Hey friends, today I want to talk about…") and waste the only seconds they had to earn attention.

Short-form has four beats. Four. Every video. Every time.

## The framework

1. **HOOK** — open with a principle statement, problem statement, or provocative claim. The first 2 seconds determine whether the second 2 seconds happen.

2. **BUILD** — set up the tension. "Most pastors think X. They're not." Or three-beat negation: "Not A. Not B. Because C." The Build creates the appetite for the reveal.

3. **PAYOFF** — deliver the reframe. The "It's not X. It's Y." moment. The single insight the video carries.

4. **INVITATION** — close the loop. A concrete next move, or a "Don't [old way]. [New way]." couplet. Never a vibes close. Never "hope this helps."

## How to use it

**Five short-form scripts per week:**

- Pick one core idea on Sunday or Monday for the week.
- Run it through the five archetypes (per the `/short-form-engine` skill).
- Each script lives inside HOOK → BUILD → PAYOFF → INVITATION.
- Film all five in one session.

**The HOOK is everything:**

- Test it cold: would someone scrolling stop on this opening?
- Hook patterns that work:
  - **Most-people setup:** "Most pastors think X. They're not."
  - **Provocative claim:** "Your team isn't the problem. You are."
  - **Question with stakes:** "Why does momentum die at week 4? Three reasons."
  - **Pattern interrupt:** "Stop writing five-point sermons. Here's why."

**Things to avoid:**

- "In today's video we're going to…"
- "Welcome back to another episode…"
- "Hey friends," "tribe," "fam," "y'all."
- Any opening that doesn't immediately create curiosity, tension, or contrast.

## Why four beats, not five or three

Three beats: no Build → the Payoff doesn't land because there's no tension to release.
Five beats: too much rope → attention drops before the Payoff.
Four is the lower bound for tension + release, and the upper bound for short-form attention.
