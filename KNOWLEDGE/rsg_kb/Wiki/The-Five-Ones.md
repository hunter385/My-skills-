## The reframe

Most coaches build complex businesses on purpose — 14 offers, 6 traffic sources, vague positioning. They think complexity equals sophistication. It doesn't. It equals confusion.

The Five Ones is the inverse move. Lock in five "ones." Run them for a year. Everything else is a distraction.

## The framework

The Five Ones force a single answer to each question:

1. **One Target Market** — who you serve.
2. **One Product** — what you sell.
3. **One Conversion Tool** — what moves a prospect to client.
4. **One Traffic Source** — where prospects come from. (Long-form + short-form counts as one channel strategy.)
5. **One Year** — the time horizon for running this configuration without changing it.

When you've held all five for a year, you've earned the right to add complexity.

## How to use it

**Selecting your Five Ones:**

- Pick the Target Market you've actually served and gotten results for (not the one you wish you served).
- Pick the Product with the best evidence of demand and delivery.
- Pick the Conversion Tool that's working, not the one that's clever.
- Pick the Traffic Source that's compounding, not the one that's trendy.
- Commit to a One Year horizon. Anything shorter is a flinch.

**Running them:**

- Every offer idea, traffic experiment, partnership pitch, or strategy shift gets the same audit: does this serve the Five Ones, or does it dilute them?
- If it dilutes them, capture for the next year. Don't execute now.
- The Five Ones become the conscience of the calendar.

## Why this works

Single-variable focus compounds. Five variables in motion at once is a coordination problem, not a strategy problem. The Five Ones turn the work into a focus discipline, not a tactics scavenger hunt.

Echoes:
