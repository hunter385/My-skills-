# **Big idea**
The healthiest businesses consistently distribute cash to stakeholders. Install a simple waterfall so you maximize owner distributions while always funding taxes, operations, and growth. Think 10x results through constraint, not complexity, per Benjamin Hardy.

# **Core principles**
1. **Act broke** — Do not let big balances sit in checking. Parkinson's Law will turn surplus into waste.
2. **Plan for rain** — Emergencies are normal. A dedicated emergency fund prevents panic decisions.
3. **Fill the buckets** — Cash should flow in a set order, like a champagne tower, from operating to the next priority bucket.

# **The buckets to create**
- **Operating Account** — Keep at least one month of operating expenses available on the first day of each month.
- **Tax Savings Account** — Savings or money market, not investments. Move a fixed percent of inflows. Typical starting point is 30 to 35 percent, confirm with your tax pro.
- **Emergency Fund** — Target 3 to 6 months of fixed operating expenses. Savings account only. Use for true emergencies.
- **Investment and Sinking Funds** — Separate named accounts for known future spends, for example annual event, building down payment, equipment, ad test fund, year end bonuses, company retreat.
- **Distribution Account** — Holds distributable cash for owners. This is how you measure business health.

# **Operating rules**
- Start each month with at least one month of Opex in checking.
- Move tax money off the table weekly or monthly.
- Fill emergency fund to 3 months first, then split new overflow 50 percent to emergency until it reaches 6 months, 50 percent to available cash for investments or distributions.
- Pay distributions from the Distribution Account. Deiss's cadence example is 80 percent of the balance at quarter end, retain 20 percent as a small buffer, then sweep the remainder annually.

# **Why the distribution account matters**
- P and L can show profit while cash is tight. Cash in the distribution account is truth.
- Pulling cash out forces discipline. If the business truly needs it later, you write a check back in, which triggers better questions about efficiency and priority.
- With partners, reinvestments can adjust equity fairly, rather than letting silent partners benefit without contributing.

# **Step by step setup**
1. **Open accounts** — Operating checking, Tax savings, Emergency savings, separate Investment and Sinking funds, Distribution account.
2. **Set your one month Opex number** — Round to a clean figure so movement rules are simple.
3. **Pick your tax percent** — Enter a standing transfer rule with your bank. Review quarterly with your CPA.
4. **Build the emergency fund** — Fill to 3 months of fixed Opex fast. If you ever tap it, refilling to 3 months is priority one. No distributions until it is back at 3 months.
5. **Add named sinking funds** — Only for known future spends. Fund them from overflow after the rules above.
6. **Fund the distribution account** — Move all remaining overflow here. Track it monthly as a headline metric.
7. **Distribute on a schedule** — Example policy. 80 percent of the distribution balance paid out at quarter end, 20 percent retained until year end sweep. If capital is needed, reinvest from owners rather than quietly consuming operating cash.

# **Metrics to track on your CEO dashboard**
- **Distributable Cash month end** and trailing 3 month average.
- **Emergency Fund months of runway** based on fixed Opex.
- **Tax Account coverage** in months of expected tax payments.
- **Sinking fund coverage vs target** for each named future spend.
