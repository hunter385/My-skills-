PRE-WORK

RSG Framework

The big picture

Most churches aren't stuck because of bad leadership.

They're stuck because the pastor is carrying too much, the team isn't self-led, and there's no system turning vision into weekly action.

That's a Pastor–Team–System problem. And it has a solution.

THE 3 PILLARS

Pastor

Every pastor has a zone — the work only they can do. The preaching. The vision. Leading the room.

Without the right structure, that work gets crowded out. Decisions that should be made elsewhere land on their desk.

Get freed to lead — more time in their unique contribution zone, less in everyone else's.

Team

Most church teams are full of hard workers. They show up. They check the boxes.

But checking boxes isn't the same as building something. Without structure, team members stay in checkbox mode.

Turn doers into builders — people who don't just complete work, they improve it and multiply it.

System

Every church has a vision. Almost none have a system that turns vision into weekly action.

So the vision gets cast on Sunday and forgotten by Wednesday. Big goals sit on whiteboards.

Close the gap — so vision doesn't just get preached, it gets executed.

THE OVERLAPS ARE THE WHOLE POINT

Fruit

Pastor + Team. When you're healthy and your team leads itself, things grow — without you having to push.

Focus

Pastor + System. With the right system, you stop putting out fires and start leading. Focus is a structure problem.

Alignment

Team + System. When your team knows the system, everyone moves the same direction. No chasing, no drift.

2x

Where all three converge. Not a hype number — the natural result of a freed pastor, a multiplying team, and a system where vision becomes execution. Double attendance. Double capacity. Double impact.

Most churches are stuck at the same number for years. That's not a growth problem. It's a Pastor–Team–System problem.

WHAT IS A GROWTH PLAN?

A Growth Plan is a systematic framework — a set of tools and rhythms — that helps you move from vision to reality. It's a living process that creates:

  • Clarity on where you are and where you're going.
  • Alignment for the team around shared priorities.
  • Focus on what's important now.
  • Accountability through to completion.

Think of it as the map and compass for the journey God has entrusted to you.

Ground Rules

How we operate in the room

One Conversation At A Time

The gold is in the room. With dynamic teams, two or three meaningful conversations can pop up at once — and we don't want to miss any of it. One conversation keeps everyone in the moment and focused on the same topic.

Unplug

Every study on multitasking points to the same reality: it's impossible (unless you're a fighter pilot). Unplug from anything that could distract. A good prep technique: do a mind sweep the day before — list everything that will try to pull your attention so you don't fear forgetting it. There will be breaks for the urgent. The key is presence. You won't need to take notes — almost all the work happens inside the digital Playbook.

Last 10% of Truth

You're on the team to bring your full perspective. Withholding what you see keeps the team from making meaningful decisions. Your perspective is valid but not always accurate — humans see about 150 degrees on their best day; the other 210 gets filled in by the people around you. Share what you see, connect it to what others see, and paint the full picture. Share with love and respect. Don't hedge everything — but tread with compassion, because everything that could be broken is connected to a person, likely one in the room.

POSSIBLE OPENINGS "I'm wondering if…" · "It seems like…" · "My experience has been…" · "Should we consider that…" · "This seems to need some work…" · "It doesn't seem like we have the right…"

Come Prayed Up — This Is Kingdom Work

This process draws out, through strategic conversation, what God has been speaking to the team in prayer. We're not just strategizing. The frameworks and tools are a duplicatable process to move from vision to implementation — not a replacement for what God is doing, but a way to steward it well.

Evaluation Standards

How we judge what's working

When we evaluate ministry, strong standards matter. Weak standards make us chase the wrong things or pat ourselves on the back too soon. Effective standards create breakthrough-level clarity — so we focus time, energy, and resources on what matters most for the Kingdom.

Ineffective Evaluation

Personal Preference: "I like it, so it must be good."

Past Success: "It worked before, so it'll work again."

Incremental Improvement: "…but it's getting better."

Comparison: "We're doing better than they are."

Anecdotal Evidence: "One person loved it, so everyone must."

Effective Evaluation

Reaching the Lost: "Is this helping us effectively reach and disciple the lost?"

Driving Double Impact: "Is this moving us toward our double-impact goals?"

Seeing Double

Executive team · the measure

In Matthew 25, Jesus shows us how to steward and grow what God entrusts to us. Each obedient servant developed a strategy to return double — and received a "well done." With that same intent, we evaluate every strategy through the lens of seeing double.

We watch all key metrics, but the number one priority is weekend attendance. If our strategies help lost people get found and found people grow as disciples, that impact shows up in progressively increasing weekend attendance. We're not growing attendance to look good — it's a reflection of our work growing God's Kingdom.

The Assessment

Church · Team · Systems

This is not a report card. It's a dashboard.

Your car's gauges don't tell you if you're a good driver — they tell you what's happening under the hood. Low fuel isn't shame. It's information. An engine light isn't failure. It's a signal to act before it gets worse.

REDNeeds attention now
YELLOWAt risk — watch it
GREENHealthy

The goal isn't a perfect score. It's an honest one — so you can move forward with clarity instead of assumptions.

Church

How effectively we reach and engage people for Jesus
  • We have a clear, compelling vision people can articulate and are rallying around.
  • Our weekend experience is designed with a ONE-aware filter — evaluating everything through the lens of the person who doesn't yet know Jesus.
  • Lost people are regularly responding to the gospel and getting baptized.
  • New people have a clear, frictionless path from first visit to being on mission with us.
  • Our church is marked by life change — and we tell those stories publicly.
  • We make disciples, not just attendees — people grow in maturity and take others with them.
  • We invest in our community and expand Kingdom impact beyond our walls.

Team

How unified, equipped, and well-led our people are
  • Staff and key volunteers are aligned with the vision and can communicate where we're going.
  • We have the right people in the right roles for the season we're in.
  • Our culture is healthy, safe, and marked by trust and honest communication.
  • We have a clear, consistent system for developing, coaching, and giving feedback.
  • Volunteers are led well — we have strong volunteer leaders, not just workers.
  • We're actively investing in the next generation of leaders inside our church.

Systems

How effectively our structure supports what we're building
  • Financial systems give leadership clear, real-time visibility.
  • We have the tools and workflows to track the people we're responsible for — and act on that data.
  • Communication systems keep the right people informed at the right time.
  • We have a clear, repeatable process to move people from first contact to full engagement.
  • Project and accountability systems ensure what we commit to actually gets done.
  • We regularly assess our systems and redesign what isn't working — we don't run on momentum alone.

Caution Lights

Manage the risks

Identify and manage the risks that could derail your plan.

1 · List Potential Caution Lights

What's gotten in the way before — and what could get in the way now? Internal habits or external pressures. Don't filter yet. Get them on the table.

WATCH OUT FOR"It'll be different this time" or "We'll just try harder." Those aren't safeguards. Be specific.

2 · Choose Your Top Three

Pick the three most likely to derail this plan. Give each a short, memorable name — one or two words. The name should sting a little.

3 · Color-Code Each One

RED — Act nowCurrently an issue. Address this week.
YELLOW — WatchMostly resolved, could resurface.
GREEN — HoldBeing managed well right now.

4 · Build a Safeguard for Each

Every caution light needs a safeguard — a specific action or system that prevents it from becoming a problem.

  • New staff rule — a standing policy that closes the gap.
  • Accountability check-ins on a set cadence.
  • New point person assigned to manage it.

5 · Schedule Your Check-Ins

Caution lights only work if you revisit them. When will you review — weekly, midpoint, end of cycle? Who brings them up? Where — team meeting, 1:1, Heartbeat call?

NON-NEGOTIABLEIf it's not on the calendar, it won't happen. Put it there before you leave this session.

DEPLOY

Structure, accountability, and rhythm that make it last.

Growth Plan Post-Work

Four steps · do them in order

Complete these four steps during your Cooldown — before your next Cycle launches. Do them in order. Each one feeds the next.

Step 1 — Lock In Your Cycle Dates

Name your dates first. You can't build a dashboard, run a meetings audit, or protect your calendar if you don't know your window.

  • When does your next Cycle start and end? (90 days)
  • When does your Cooldown start and end? (30 days before the Cycle)
Cycle 1CooldownCycle 2Cooldown
Jan – MarAprilMay – JulAugust
  1. Open your calendar.
  2. Block the Cycle window (90 days). Label it: Cycle # — start to end.
  3. Block the Cooldown window (30 days before). Label it: Cooldown # — start to end.
  4. Share those dates with your leadership team today.

Step 2 — Set Up Your Cycle Rock Dashboard

Your central source of truth. It answers three questions at a glance: What matters most? Are we on track? How far along are we? Without it, rocks fade and urgency fills the gap.

GREENOn track to complete by end of Cycle
YELLOWPossible, but not guaranteed
REDStuck. Need help.
  1. Pull your Growth Plan. Identify your 5–10 Cycle Rocks.
  2. Decide where you'll keep the dashboard.
  3. Create columns: Rock Name · Owner · Status · Progress % · Target Date.
  4. Enter each rock. Assign one owner per rock.
  5. Share the dashboard with your team.
  6. Schedule a recurring 5-minute slot in your weekly staff meeting — update it live, in the room.
THE WEEKLY UPDATE IS NON-NEGOTIABLEEach owner gives a status (R/Y/G). That's it. Problems get solved offline — not in the update.

Step 3 — Set Up Your Ideas List

Every new idea mid-Cycle is a threat to your current rocks — even good ones. The Ideas List is where ideas go to wait. It protects current work without killing future thinking.

THE RULENothing moves from the Ideas List into execution until the next Cycle.
  1. Create a shared document titled "Ideas List."
  2. Add three columns: Idea · Suggested By · Date Added.
  3. Add a divider: Past Cycle (didn't make it) and New (came up mid-cycle).
  4. Share it with your leadership team.
  5. Communicate the ground rule: adding to the list means it's out of scope this Cycle.
  6. During Cooldown, review the list before your Growth Plan session. Promote the best into next cycle's rocks.
MODEL THE BEHAVIORWhen someone brings an idea mid-Cycle, say: "That's good. Let's put it on the Ideas List and revisit it in the Cooldown." Then do it.

Step 4 — Do a Meetings Audit

You can have clear rocks, an updated dashboard, and a strong Ideas List — and still finish nothing if the calendar doesn't reflect your priorities. The audit answers one question: Does your calendar reflect what you say matters most? If no, the calendar changes before the Cycle starts.

  • Add — meetings missing but required for new priorities.
  • Subtract — meetings that no longer serve the mission. Kill them.
  • Trim — meetings that need less time or frequency.
  • Fix — meetings that stay but need redesign.
  1. Watch the Meetings Audit lesson.
  2. Test each recurring meeting and adjust where needed.

Summary Checklist

  • Step 1 — Cycle and Cooldown dates locked and shared with the team.
  • Step 2 — Cycle Rock Dashboard live with all rocks, owners, and dates.
  • Step 3 — Ideas List created and the ground rule communicated.
  • Step 4 — Meetings Audit done and calendar changes implemented.
When all four are done, your team starts the Cycle with clarity — not urgency.
