# A-Team Workshop — Full Build

**Format:** Virtual (Zoom), 90 minutes
**Teachers:** Hunter (lead), Mark, Scott
**Scope:** Front Door (Hiring) + Back Door (Firing)

---

## Workshop Overview

Most pastors think they have a people problem. They don't. They have a *door* problem. The wrong people get in because there's no system at the front door. The wrong people stay too long because there's no system at the back door. This workshop installs the discipline at both doors — Hire for the Five + the 90+ Rule at the front, the 7 C's of Healthy Separation at the back — and ends with pastors having scored a real hire and drafted the script for the conversation they've been avoiding. The thesis: an A-Team isn't built by trying harder. It's built by closing both doors.

# WHY — The Door Problem
*(0:03–0:12 — Hunter, 9 minutes)*

> "Let me name what most of you are living with right now.
>
> Most pastors I talk to have a staff that looks fine on paper — and feels heavy in real life.
>
> You walk into the office on Monday and you can feel it. The energy is off. Someone's underperforming and everyone knows but nobody's saying. Or someone's leaving and you're already dreading the conversation. Or you have an empty seat that's been open six months and every résumé that comes across your desk feels like a maybe.
>
> So you do what most pastors do. You pray about it. You read another leadership book. You try harder. You give one more talk to the team about culture and ownership and execution.
>
> And nothing changes.
>
> Because the problem isn't out there. The problem is that the team has no system at the doors."

*(Beat.)*

**[Problem Beat 1 — Symptom]**

> "Here's the symptom most of you are seeing:
>
> You hire on a feeling. You fire on guilt.
>
> The interview goes well. The candidate's a great communicator. They love Jesus. Their references say nice things. You feel good walking out of the room and you offer them the job.
>
> Eight months later you're sitting across from them again. Same person. Different conversation. And now you're trying to figure out how to let them go without blowing up the church, the family, or the friendship — and you can't sleep at night because nothing about how this is going to land feels clean.
>
> That's the cycle most of you are stuck in. Hired on a feeling. Fired on guilt. Repeat."

*(Beat.)*

**[Problem Beat 2 — Real Cause]**

> "But here's what's actually happening underneath.
>
> You don't have a hiring problem. You don't have a firing problem.
>
> You have a *clarity* problem.
>
> Nobody wrote down what 'right' looked like before the interview. Nobody decided what a 'no' looked like in advance. Nobody named what the role needed to deliver in 90 days. So when the candidate showed up, the only thing you had to evaluate them with was your gut.
>
> And then on the back end — nobody decided what 'no longer the right fit' actually meant in writing. So when the moment came, you didn't have a process. You had an emotion. And every staff exit happens out of emotion or out of clarity. Pick one."

*(Beat.)*

**[Diagnosis]**

> "The real diagnosis is this. Your team is not a hiring or firing problem. It is a *system* problem at the front and back door.
>
> A team that runs on emotion gets the people emotion brings them. And emotion always brings you the same person.
>
> A team that runs on a system gets the team the system protects.
>
> An A-Team is not the team you wish you had. It is the team your doors let in and your doors honor on the way out."

**Punch close (dropped alone):**

> *You don't have a people problem. You have a door problem.*

# WHAT — THE FRONT DOOR
*(0:12–0:32 — Mark, 20 minutes)*

> "Hunter's going to hand it to me now, because I want to walk you through what we install at the front door.
>
> The front door is where most of the pain begins — but almost none of you actually have a system here. You have a job description, a couple of interviews, and a gut feeling. That's not a system. That's a guess in a suit.
>
> What we install at the front door is two frameworks that work together: **Hire for the Five** and **the 90+ Rule.** One tells you *what to look for.* The other tells you *what to do with what you find.*"

---

## The Definition

> "Let me give you the definition first.
>
> **The Front Door is a written hiring discipline where every candidate is scored against five non-negotiables and one decision rule — before any offer is made.**
>
> Not interviewed. Scored. In writing. By more than one person. Before the offer. Every time."

---

## Component 1 — Hire for the Five

> "The Five C's are not a checklist. They are five filters you score the candidate through. Every one of them gets a number.
>
> **1. Competency.** They have *demonstrated* the skill the role requires. Not 'I think they could do it.' Show me where they already did it.
>
> *Hiring on potential without proof is how you end up with a youth pastor who's a great preacher and can't run a small group rotation to save his life.*
>
> **2. Character.** Their private life is consistent with their public life. The pattern nobody is asking about is the pattern that will eventually surface.
>
> *Strong testimony does not equal strong character. Testimony is what they tell you. Character is what's true when nobody's watching.*
>
> **3. Chemistry.** Not with you. With the *team.* Does the room get better when this person walks in?
>
> *Pastor-only chemistry checks are how you hire the person you love and your staff resents within 60 days.*
>
> **4. Capacity.** Can they grow into the *next* version of this role? Not just today's.
>
> *Hiring people who are already maxed out is how you cap your church before it even grows.*
>
> **5. Culture.** They already live your values. You're not going to teach this. You're going to confirm it.
>
> *Hiring 'good people' and then trying to rewire them is the slowest, most expensive mistake in church staffing.*"

**Paired contrast:**

> "*Skill without character builds a ministry that breaks at the worst possible time. Character without skill builds a ministry that breaks slowly — and quietly. The Five C's catch both.*"

---

## Component 2 — The 90+ Rule

> "Now you've scored five C's. Here's what most pastors do next: they stare at the scores, frown, talk themselves into it, and make the offer.
>
> The 90+ Rule is what stops that.
>
> **The Rule:**
>
> - You score the Five C's, and five more behavioral checks — Calling, Coachability, Conviction, Consistency, Confidence — each 1 to 10. Ten total.
> - You need a **90 or higher** to extend an offer.
> - **Any single score below a 7 = automatic no.** Even if the total clears 90.
> - **Character and Culture are weighted double.** Non-negotiables.
> - Everyone who interviews fills out their own scorecard. In writing. In pen.
>
> Below 90, you don't hire. You hold the seat.
>
> Because the cost of the wrong hire is always higher than the cost of the empty chair."

**Physical metaphor:**

> "Think of it like the 10-items-or-less lane at the grocery store. The line is painted. Most people self-regulate when the line is clearly painted. The same is true of your candidates and your team. When the line is faint, everyone freelances. When the line is bright, almost everyone gets in line — or recognizes they're not in the right lane."

**Punch close (dropped alone):**

> *Score it. Or you'll suffer it.*

# WHAT — THE BACK DOOR
*(0:32–0:50 — Scott, 18 minutes)*

> "Mark just gave you the front door. I'm taking you to the back.
>
> Here's the truth most pastors don't want to hear. **Most of your stress about your staff is actually grief about a conversation you haven't had yet.**
>
> You know who needs to go. You've known for months. You've prayed about it. You've avoided it. And every Monday morning, the weight of it sits on your chest before your feet hit the floor.
>
> The back door isn't cruel. The back door is *kind* when it's done well — because **clarity is kinder than confusion.** What's cruel is making someone live in a role they're failing at because you don't have the courage or the system to honor them out."

---

## The Definition

> "Here's the definition:
>
> **The Back Door is a seven-step discipline that allows a leader to honor a person, protect the team, and close a season cleanly — without drama, guilt, or chaos.**
>
> Not a firing. A *separation*. There's a difference, and you'll feel it in the room."

---

## The 7 C's of a Healthy Separation

> "Let me walk you through the seven. I'm going to be tight because each one could be its own session.
>
> **1. Center yourself first.** You don't enter the room to vent, punish, dominate, or win. You enter to bring clarity, honor the person, and close the season. If you walk in angry, you'll do damage you can't undo.
>
> **2. Confirm the decision before the meeting.** *Do not* process the decision inside the room. The meeting is not where you decide. The meeting is where you deliver. If you walk in unsure, you create chaos.
>
> **3. Communicate clearly and quickly.** Don't bury the lead. Don't build twenty minutes of tension. Three short sentences, then stop talking. Let them feel it. *Most leaders talk too much in this conversation because they're the uncomfortable one.*
>
> **4. Honor the person without reversing the decision.** Affirm character, calling, contribution. Do not affirm in ways that create confusion. 'Maybe someday we can revisit this' is not honor. It's emotional instability dressed up like grace.
>
> **5. Handle HR and practicals with calm clarity.** Final pay, severance, benefits, equipment, communication plan — *prepared in advance.* Never improvise severance in the room. Never.
>
> **6. Care for the spouse and family.** Acknowledge the family is impacted. Offer specific, finite support. Do not over-pastor, overpromise, or emotionally entangle.
>
> **7. Close with honor and finality.** End with grace. Not with hope you don't have. Not with a door you're not actually leaving open. Clarity, kindness, finality — that's the close."

---

## The Order of Communication

> "And here's the piece almost everyone gets wrong — the order *after* the conversation.
>
> **Board → Staff member → Most impacted volunteers → Their team → Stop.**
>
> No church-wide announcement. No tribute reel. No 'pray for them' moment from the stage. Dignified silence with the broader church is almost always healthier than a public production. *Grief without chaos. Honor without spectacle.*"

**Paired contrast:**

> "*A vague exit done with feeling protects nobody and hurts everyone. A clear exit done with honor protects the person, the team, and the church — all at once.*"

**Punch close (dropped alone):**

> *Clarity is kinder than confusion.*

# HOW — The Live Application
*(0:50–1:20 — Hunter facilitates, Mark and Scott coach in chat + breakouts. 30 minutes.)*

> "Mark and Scott just gave you the systems. Now we're going to do the work in the room.
>
> Open the worksheet in your chat. We're going to do three things in the next 30 minutes. By the time you close your laptop, you will have *scored* a real hire on the 90+ Rule, and you will have *drafted* the opening three sentences of the conversation you've been avoiding.
>
> This is not homework. This is the workout."

---

### **Step 1: The Front Door Score**
*Facilitator instruction: Hunter walks them into it. Mark drops the scorecard graphic in chat. Set a 12-minute timer on screen.*

> **Participant prompt (worksheet):**
>
> *"Pick one person on your staff right now — current hire, recent hire, or someone you're about to offer the job to. Score them on all ten C's, 1 to 10, in pen on this sheet. Don't talk to anyone yet. Don't soften the score. Don't round up. Just score what's actually true.*
>
> *1. Competency        ___ /10*
> *2. Character (×2)    ___ /10*
> *3. Chemistry         ___ /10*
> *4. Capacity          ___ /10*
> *5. Culture (×2)      ___ /10*
> *6. Calling           ___ /10*
> *7. Coachability      ___ /10*
> *8. Conviction        ___ /10*
> *9. Consistency       ___ /10*
> *10. Confidence       ___ /10*
>
> *Total: ___ / 100  (Character and Culture count twice — so max is 120, you're aiming for 108+ on the doubled scale, or 90+ on the unweighted.)*
>
> *Any score under 7? Circle it. That's an automatic flag, regardless of total.*
>
> *Now answer one question in writing: What did this exercise just tell you about this hire that you didn't want to admit before?"*
>
> **Time: 12 minutes** *(2 min orientation + 8 min scoring + 2 min written reflection)*

---

### **Step 2: The Back Door Draft**
*Facilitator instruction: Hunter introduces it, Scott pins the 7 C's framework in chat. Set a 15-minute timer.*

> **Participant prompt (worksheet):**
>
> *"Name the conversation you've been avoiding. One person. One conversation. The one that keeps showing up in your head on the drive home.*
>
> *Now, using the 7 C's, draft just the opening of that conversation. Not the whole thing. The first 90 seconds. In writing.*
>
> *Write out, word for word, what you would say in the first three sentences:*
>
> *Sentence 1 (Set the tone):* ________________________________
>
> *Sentence 2 (Name the reality clearly):* ________________________________
>
> *Sentence 3 (Deliver the decision or the next step):* ________________________________
>
> *Now circle anything in your draft that is:*
> *— Vague (would the person know exactly what you mean?)*
> *— Defensive (are you justifying instead of leading?)*
> *— Hopeful in a way that creates confusion (are you leaving a door open you don't intend to leave open?)*
>
> *Cross those out. Rewrite them straight."*
>
> **Time: 15 minutes** *(3 min orientation + 10 min drafting + 2 min self-edit)*

---

### **Step 3: The Pair Share**
*Facilitator instruction: Hunter sends to breakouts, 2 per room. Mark and Scott rotate.*

> **Participant prompt (in breakout):**
>
> *"You have 3 minutes. Read your three sentences to your partner. Your partner gets 60 seconds to give you one note: 'too soft,' 'too sharp,' or 'land it.' Then switch."*
>
> **Time: 3 minutes**

---

# NOW — The Close
*(1:20–1:30 — Hunter, 10 minutes including any final Q+A pulled tight)*

> "Here's where we land.
>
> Before you close this laptop today — not tomorrow, not next cycle — do one thing.
>
> Take the three sentences you just drafted. Put them in your phone, in your notes, with a date attached. The date is when you are going to *say* them.
>
> That's the one action step. One person. One conversation. One date. In your phone before this session ends.
>
> If you can't pick a date, then the conversation isn't real to you yet — and the weight you're carrying is just going to keep showing up on the drive home. The only way to put it down is to set the date."

*(Beat.)*

> "Most of you walked in here today thinking the A-Team is the team you wish you had.
>
> The A-Team is the team your doors let in and the team your doors honor on the way out. Both doors. Closed clean.
>
> You don't need more leadership content. You need to close the doors."

**Punch close (dropped alone — final line of the workshop):**

> *Win the doors. Win the team.*

# FACILITATOR NOTES

### When the room is with you
- Cameras on. Chat filling up during the WHY — especially around the line "hired on a feeling, fired on guilt."
- Visible reactions during the Front Door Score. Some nods, some sighs. That's the framework landing.
- Breakouts come back quiet. Quiet is good here. Quiet means they wrote something real.

### When the room is losing you
- Chat goes flat during the WHAT sections. That means the teaching is drifting toward concept and away from pain. Pull it back to one specific example fast.
- During the live application, if nobody is writing — that means the prompt is too abstract. Read it aloud again, slowly, and tell them *exactly* what should be on their page in 30 seconds.
- If cameras go off, you've lost them. Pause. Ask one direct question in the chat: "Who can name the person they're scoring right now? Drop their first name only." Re-engage.

### Most common pushback — and how to handle it
The objection: **"My team is too small for this — I only have 2 paid staff and 30 volunteers."**

Hunter's response (have this ready, do not improvise):
> "Then this is the most important workshop you'll attend. Small teams suffer the wrong hire the longest. You don't need a system because your team is big. You need a system because your team is small — there's nowhere to hide a bad hire."

### Three-teacher coordination notes
- **Hunter opens, frames the why, runs the live app, and closes.** Mark and Scott teach the two WHAT sections and serve as floating coaches in chat and breakouts during the HOW.
- **Mark hands to Scott** with one line: *"Mark just gave you the front door. I'm taking you to the back."* No filler in the transitions.
- **All three are visible in the chat during Live Application.** Coaching questions get answered fast — under 60 seconds.

### One thing to cut if running long
Cut Step 3 (the Pair Share breakout). It's the most disposable. The score and the draft are the workout. The pair share is the bonus rep.

### One thing to add if the room is hot
After the close, offer this: *"If you want one of us to look at your three sentences before you say them, drop them in the post-session form. We'll send a 60-second voice memo back to the first 10 we get."* Costs you 30 min. Builds enormous trust.
