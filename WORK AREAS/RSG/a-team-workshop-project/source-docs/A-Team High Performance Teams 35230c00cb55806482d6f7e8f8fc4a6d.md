## Hiring

- **The 5 C's of Hiring -** What to Look for Before You Bring Someone Onto Your Team
    
    The people you have on your team will make or break you.
    
    Look for the Five C’s
    
    a. Competency- they have the skills
    
    b. Character-they have the heart
    
    c. Chemistry- they fit on the team
    
    d. Capacity-they can grow with us
    
    e. Culture-they have similar convictions
    
    Leaders first, specialist second- people developers more than anything
    
    - I want to always be in the process of developing more and more potential staff…..
    - Give opportunities for key volunteers to grow and who you what they can do
    
- **How to create a Preferred Profile for hiring -** Build a Clear Picture of the Ideal Team Member Before You Hire
    
    Here are some ideas you could pull together to build a strong **Preferred Profile** template for hiring. I'd structure it as a one-pager (or two columns) that forces clarity *before* anyone gets interviewed.
    
    ## Core Sections
    
    **1. Role Snapshot**
    
    - Position title
    - Reports to / who reports to them
    - Full-time / part-time / contract
    - Cycle this role serves (which Cycle Rock are they unlocking?)
    - The pain this hire is solving (one sentence)
    
    **2. The "Why Now"**
    
    - What's broken or stuck without this person
    - What "win" looks like 90 days in
    - What "win" looks like 1 year in
    
    **3. The 5 C's Scorecard** (tying back to your existing teaching)
    
    - Competency — non-negotiable skills (list 3–5)
    - Character — heart/integrity markers
    - Chemistry — team fit signals
    - Capacity — growth ceiling we need
    - Culture — convictions and values they must share
    
    *Score each 1–10 in interviews — must score 90+ overall.*
    
    **4. The 3 Goals / 6 Responsibilities / 5 Values (their future 365)**
    
    - Draft the 365 *before* you hire — this becomes the role's north star and the basis for evaluation.
    
    **5. "Who We Think We Need"** (left column)
    
    - Top 3 outcomes this role must deliver
    - Spiritual maturity markers
    - Leadership level (worker / equipper / leader of leaders)
    - Stage-of-life fit (margin, season, family)
    - Theological alignment
    - Deal-breakers / red flags
    
    **6. "Who They Think They Are"** (right column — given to candidate)
    
    - Same questions reflected back so they self-assess
    - Their story / calling
    - Their strengths and weaknesses (in their own words)
    - Why this role, why this church, why now
    
    **7. Compensation & Logistics**
    
    - Salary range
    - Benefits / housing / sabbatical
    - Office vs remote expectations
    - Travel / weekend expectations
    
    **8. Sourcing Plan**
    
    - Internal candidates to consider (promote when proven)
    - External pipeline (network, posting locations)
    - Referral asks
    
    **9. Decision Making Matrix Hookup**
    
    - Pre-define the weighted categories you'll score in
    - Set the 90+ threshold and minimum thresholds per C
    
    **10. Timeline & Process**
    
    - Phase 1: Application + 365 self-fill
    - Phase 2: Phone screen
    - Phase 3: In-person interview + team chemistry
    - Phase 4: Spouse meeting (if applicable)
    - Phase 5: Reference checks
    - Phase 6: Probationary period terms

- **The Decision Making Matrix -** The Leadership Tool for Navigating Staffing Decisions
    
    Here are some ideas for a **Top 10 Questions** list that powers the Decision Making Matrix. Each one is scored 1–10, totaling 100 — and you're looking for 90+ to move forward. I'll give you 10 strong candidates plus a few alternates you can swap in depending on the role.
    
    ## The Core 10 (Scored 1–10 Each)
    
    **1. Competency — Can they actually do the job?**
    
    *Have they demonstrated the specific skills this role requires, not just adjacent ones?*
    
    **2. Character — Would I trust them with my family, my finances, and my reputation?**
    
    *Is their private life consistent with their public life?*
    
    **3. Chemistry — Does the team get better when they walk in the room?**
    
    *Energy goes up, not down. Existing staff want to work with them.*
    
    **4. Capacity — Can they grow into the next version of this role?**
    
    *Are they a ceiling or a floor? Will they outgrow what we're hiring them for?*
    
    **5. Culture — Do they already live our values without being asked?**
    
    *Not "Can they adapt?" but "Are they already this kind of person?"*
    
    **6. Calling — Is this a job to them or a calling?**
    
    *Are they pursuing the paycheck, the title, or the mission?*
    
    **7. Coachability — Do they receive feedback like a gift or a threat?**
    
    *Look at how they describe past correction, bosses, or losses.*
    
    **8. Conviction — Are they theologically and philosophically aligned with us?**
    
    *Will they preach what we preach, even when no one's watching?*
    
    **9. Consistency — Do their references, past patterns, and current behavior tell the same story?**
    
    *Three references, three identical themes = green light. Different stories = red flag.*
    
    **10. Confidence (Gut Check) — When I imagine them in this role for 5 years, do I feel peace or pressure?**
    
    *The Holy Spirit check. If you have to talk yourself into them, the answer is no.*
    
    ---
    
    ## Alternate Questions (Swap In When Useful)
    
    - **Conflict** — How do they handle disagreement? (with peers, with you, with spouses)
    - **Cash** — Is their financial situation healthy enough that they can serve without being controlled by money?
    - **Commitment** — Have they finished what they started in past roles?
    - **Cost** — Will hiring them require shrinking something or someone else?
    - **Catalytic** — Do they bring momentum, or do they need momentum brought to them?
    - **Capability with Conflict** — Can they have hard conversations without freezing or fighting?
    - **Clarity** — Do they communicate cleanly, or do you leave conversations confused?
    - **Curiosity** — Do they ask good questions about the church and the role?
    - **Cadence** — Does their pace match our pace?
    - **Calling Confirmed by Spouse** — Does their spouse light up about this role too?
    
    ---
    
    ## Scoring Format Ideas
    
    - **Simple 1–10 next to each question** — total it up, must hit 90+
    - **Weighted version** — give Character and Culture 2x weight (they're non-negotiables)
    - **Color bands**: 90–100 green (hire), 75–89 yellow (probably no), under 75 red (hard no)
    - **Tie-breaker rule**: any single score below a 7 = automatic no, even if the total is 90+
    
    ---
    
    ## Activation Ideas (For the Workshop)
    
    - Give attendees a real recent hire and have them score them retroactively — discuss what they would've caught
    - Give them a fillable scorecard PDF to use the next time they interview
    - Show how the Preferred Profile feeds *directly* into the Matrix (the C's connect cleanly)
    - Teach the "below a 7 = automatic no" rule as the discipline most leaders skip

## Firing

- **The 7 C’s of a Healthy Separation Guide -** The Step-by-Step Framework for Leading Healthy Staff Transitions
    
    # 1. CENTER YOURSELF FIRST
    
    ### 💬 “Am I emotionally prepared to lead this well?”
    
    Before the meeting:
    
    - Pray.
    - Remove frustration.
    - Remove the need to justify yourself emotionally.
    - Remove sarcasm.
    - Remove accumulated resentment.
    - Remove the desire to “teach them a lesson.”
    
    You are not entering the room to:
    
    - vent,
    - punish,
    - dominate,
    - or win an argument.
    
    You are entering to:
    
    - bring clarity,
    - honor the person,
    - steward the organization,
    - and close the season cleanly.
    
    ### Important Internal Posture
    
    > “This is painful, but clarity is kinder than prolonging misalignment.”
    > 
    
    ---
    
    # 2. CONFIRM THE DECISION BEFORE THE MEETING
    
    ### 💬 “Do not process the decision inside the meeting.”
    
    One of the most damaging mistakes leaders make:
    
    They enter the conversation emotionally uncertain.
    
    The meeting becomes:
    
    - confusing,
    - mixed-signal,
    - overly emotional,
    - or negotiable.
    
    If the decision is made:
    
    - make it before the meeting,
    - with proper leadership and HR alignment,
    - and enter calmly.
    
    This is not:
    
    > “Let’s see how this conversation goes.”
    > 
    
    This is:
    
    > “We have determined this role is no longer the right fit.”
    > 
    
    That clarity actually reduces trauma.
    
    ---
    
    # 3. COMMUNICATE CLEARLY AND QUICKLY
    
    ### 💬 “Don’t bury the lead.”
    
    Do not spend 20 minutes building tension.
    
    Do not:
    
    - overly compliment,
    - over-spiritualize,
    - re-litigate the PIP,
    - or emotionally circle the runway.
    
    Get there quickly.
    
    Something like:
    
    > “Thank you for meeting with us. I know these conversations are difficult.”
    > 
    
    Pause.
    
    > “Over this last season, we’ve worked together through a focused improvement process and had multiple conversations around role expectations and alignment.”
    > 
    
    Pause.
    
    > “At this point, we’ve concluded that this role is no longer the right fit, and today will be your final day on staff.”
    > 
    
    Then stop talking.
    
    Let them feel it.
    
    Most leaders talk too much because THEY are uncomfortable.
    
    ---
    
    # 4. HONOR THE PERSON WITHOUT REVERSING THE DECISION
    
    This is crucial.
    
    You can affirm:
    
    - character,
    - effort,
    - calling,
    - value,
    - contributions,
    - love,
    - history.
    
    But do NOT affirm in ways that create confusion.
    
    Avoid:
    
    > “Maybe someday we can revisit this…”
    > 
    
    Avoid:
    
    > “I’m not sure this is right either…”
    > 
    
    Avoid:
    
    > “This kills me more than you…”
    > 
    
    That creates emotional instability.
    
    Instead:
    
    > “This decision is not a statement about your worth or your value to the Kingdom.”
    > 
    
    > “We are grateful for the ways you’ve served here.”
    > 
    
    > “But we no longer believe this role is the best fit moving forward.”
    > 
    
    Clear.
    
    Kind.
    
    Steady.
    
    ---
    
    # 5. HANDLE HR AND PRACTICALS WITH CALM CLARITY
    
    This is where churches often become chaotic.
    
    ### You need structure.
    
    Ideally:
    
    - one direct leader,
    - one HR/admin person,
    - minimal people in the room.
    
    Not a tribunal.
    
    ---
    
    # HR Topics to Cover
    
    ## Final Pay
    
    Explain:
    
    - final paycheck,
    - PTO payout,
    - benefits end date,
    - insurance details,
    - retirement info.
    
    ---
    
    ## Severance
    
    Churches should think carefully about severance.
    
    Not because you “owe” it emotionally.
    
    But because:
    
    - transitions are difficult,
    - ministry jobs are unique,
    - churches should model generosity when possible.
    
    ### Typical Healthy Severance Approaches
    
    | Situation | Common Range |
    | --- | --- |
    | Under 1 year | 2–4 weeks |
    | 1–3 years | 1–2 months |
    | Long-term/key leader | 2–6 months |
    
    This depends on:
    
    - finances,
    - circumstances,
    - conduct,
    - legal advice,
    - church policy.
    
    ---
    
    ### IMPORTANT:
    
    Never improvise severance emotionally in the meeting.
    
    Have:
    
    - numbers finalized,
    - documents prepared,
    - approvals completed beforehand.
    
    ---
    
    ## Transition Expectations
    
    Clarify:
    
    - laptop/equipment,
    - email access,
    - office access,
    - announcement timing,
    - communication plan.
    
    Keep it dignified.
    
    Do not treat them like a criminal unless there is actual misconduct.
    
    ---
    
    # 6. TALKING ABOUT THEIR SPOUSE AND FAMILY
    
    This is where pastors feel tremendous emotional pressure.
    
    Especially in church culture.
    
    You may deeply love their spouse and kids.
    
    But be careful not to:
    
    - over-pastor,
    - overpromise,
    - or emotionally entangle.
    
    You can say:
    
    > “We recognize this affects your family, not just you.”
    > 
    
    > “We do not take that lightly.”
    > 
    
    > “We want to handle this with as much honor and care as possible.”
    > 
    
    If appropriate:
    
    - offer counseling support,
    - transition coaching,
    - resume help,
    - references (if truthful and appropriate),
    - prayer.
    
    But don’t create false hope.
    
    ---
    
    # Should You Talk to the Spouse?
    
    Usually:
    
    - not before the employee,
    - not separately without permission,
    - not in a way that triangulates.
    
    However:
    
    - after the meeting,
    - depending on relationship,
    - it may be appropriate to offer a pastoral conversation with both of them.
    
    Especially if:
    
    - they are deeply embedded in the church,
    - the spouse is hurt/confused,
    - or relational repair matters long term.
    
    But this should feel pastoral, not defensive.
    
    ---
    
    # 7. CLOSE WITH HONOR AND FINALITY
    
    The ending matters enormously.
    
    You are trying to create:
    
    - grief without chaos,
    - sadness without humiliation,
    - clarity without cruelty.
    
    Something like:
    
    > “I know this is painful.”
    > 
    
    > “Thank you for the ways you’ve served and invested here.”
    > 
    
    > “We genuinely want God’s best for you moving forward.”
    > 
    
    > “We believe clarity is the most honoring thing we can give you right now.”
    > 
    
    Then explain next steps simply.
    
    No excessive preaching.
    
    No overexplaining.
    
    No trying to make them feel okay immediately.
    
    ---
    
    # 8. THE ORDER OF COMMUNICATION AFTER THE DECISION
    
    ### 💬 “Who hears what, when, and from whom?”
    
    The most common place a staff exit goes sideways is *after* the meeting — when communication runs ahead of leadership. Stay disciplined about the order.
    
    **1. The Board — before anything else.**
    
    No exit happens without alignment. Get clarity, get approval, get prayer cover before you ever sit down with the staff member.
    
    **2. The staff — immediately after the conversation.**
    
    Same day, ideally same hour. They should never hear it secondhand or through the rumor cycle.
    
    **3. The most influential and most impacted.**
    
    Key volunteers, ministry leaders, and close relationships of the departing staff member. They deserve a direct conversation, not a hallway whisper.
    
    **4. The team or department they led.**
    
    Walk them through what’s changing, who is covering responsibilities, and when next steps happen.
    
    **5. Stop there.**
    
    ---
    
    ### What NOT to do
    
    - Do **not** announce to the whole church.
    - Do **not** throw a church-wide “thank you” party.
    - Do **not** make it a stage moment.
    - Do **not** post a tribute on social media on their behalf.
    
    Most staff transitions do not require a congregation-wide announcement. Dignified silence with the broader church is almost always healthier than a public production.
    
    > “Grief without chaos. Honor without spectacle.”
    > 
    
    ---
    
    # A Few Things Churches MUST Avoid
    
    ## 1. Spiritualizing the Decision
    
    Avoid:
    
    > “God told us…”
    > 
    
    Avoid:
    
    > “We just don’t have peace…”
    > 
    
    Avoid:
    
    > “You’re released from this house…”
    > 
    
    That language can become spiritually manipulative and deeply damaging.
    
    Speak concretely.
    
    ---
    
    ## 2. Over-Explaining
    
    Once the decision is final:
    
    - don’t debate,
    - don’t pile on evidence,
    - don’t build a legal case verbally.
    
    You already did that during the improvement process.
    
    ---
    
    ## 3. Making It About Loyalty
    
    Never imply:
    
    - betrayal,
    - disloyalty,
    - lack of submission,
    unless that truly was the issue and was clearly documented.
    
    ---
    
    ## 4. Public Shame
    
    Protect dignity.
    
    Staff departures should not become:
    
    - sermon illustrations,
    - gossip,
    - vague “pray for them” moments,
    - or leadership speculation.
    
    ---

    # The 6 C’s
    
    - **1. CONFESSION**
        
         **💬** I may have missed it
        
        ---
        
        - Where have I been unclear about expectations?
        - Did we define success in writing?
        - Have I tolerated drift that I should have addressed earlier?
        - Did I provide consistent feedback and reinforcement?
        - What do I need to own before addressing performance?
        
        First thing I wanna do is start with confession and admittance. This sets the stage that I’m coming in with a bowed knee and willing to serve the person. The scripture says that we are to serve one another. And serve one another out of reverence for Christ. By initiating this convo, I’m attempting to serve this person.
        
        I want to help them accomplish all that God has put in their heart. I want them to be able to do Colossians 3:23 and be in a role that fits the 5S Model. So this convo is about me serving them, not them serving me and my vision - although I have one. 
        
        We’re doing this because I want the best for them and am now wondering whether this is the best. They have work to do in the Kingdom and we’re confirming or disconfirming that their current role is part of that work.
        
        This step is especially important if I have allowed or tolerated a certain way of operating or behavior and have not had a formal meeting with written instructions to deal with it. If an issue has been languishing, then it’s my fault, not theirs.
        
        So, if possible, first thing is to admit or confess that somehow up to this point, you have not been clear or have not painted the lines clearly enough for the person to understand how to move forward.
        
    - **2. CLARIFICATION**
        
        **💬** Are we on the same page?
        
        ---
        
        - What were the original goals or responsibilities?
        - What does the role require right now?
        - Where specifically are we missing the mark?
        - What standards or values are being compromised?
        - What evidence or examples illustrate the gap?
        
        This might be difficult if you don’t use 365 or you’re not typically a written culture. It’s very important to have a written track record of conversations/decisions so that we can manage against them. At this point, there shouldn’t be any question on what you agreed upon.
        
        **But if there is** - because it was vaguely documented or not documented at all. You can say:
        
        > “This is what I thought we had previously agreed upon on your 365. Does that seem right to you?”
        > 
        
        Note: If there is a discrepancy, then this is another great place to confess:
        
        > “Well, our gap between what you thought and what I thought is exactly why we’re having this meeting. Maybe I haven’t been clear. So going forward we’ll make sure to put in writing what we’re agreeing upon. With that said, here’s what I want…
        > 
        
        There are a couple of things that can happen here depending on how clear you’ve been in the past:
        
        1. If unclear - You can re-clarify what you desire in this role and execution.
            1. Have it ready before the meeting so you don’t have to ad lib
        2. If clear - You can share what you perceive to be the gap
        
        > “For this next cycle, we need to see movement from red to at least yellow, ideally green, in these specific areas.”
        > 
        
        Name them specifically:
        
        - This responsibility
        - This value
        - This goal
        
        Paint the lines clearly. Driving on a highway with faintly painted lines is daunting.
        
        ***Clarity is Kindness!***
        
        Most decent leaders that I know - if the lines are painted clearly - will self regulate. They’ll get in the line or recognize that the lines do not work for them. It’s like the 10-item lane at the grocery store.
        
        Sometimes there’s a low score on a responsibility or an unfinished goal. Something didn’t get done or it’s in the yellow or red zone. If that’s the case then clearly reminding of the goal/responsibility and then showing the distance between what is desired and what is delivered is key.
        
        This step is about getting on the same page.
        
    - **3. CONVERSATION**
        
        **💬** How are you seeing this?
        
        ---
        
        - How do you feel you are performing right now?
        - What obstacles are getting in your way?
        - What support do you feel you are lacking?
        - Where do you believe expectations are unclear?
        - What do you think needs to change?
        
        Then at that point I will allow for some feedback. The “Do you understand” question isn’t necessarily my favorite. Something a little more open ended like **“how are you seeing this?” Or “what are you sensing here?” Or “is there something that I may be missing in this?”**
        
        Especially if I have not been meeting with them on the regular, I want to leave it open to the possibility that I may not be seeing it correctly or may not have all of the information.
        
        This is a moment to test the waters and see if we’re tracking together now.
        
        If I have been regularly meeting with them and setting written goals, then there may be very little to gather in this step.
        
    - **4. CONFIRMATION**
        
        **💬** Design and agree upon a written plan
        
        ---
        
        - What specific behaviors must change?
        - What measurable outcomes define success?
        - What are the deadlines or checkpoints?
        - What support or resources will be provided?
        - What happens if the plan is met? Not met?
        
        After their feedback, I want us to determine the game plan going forward.
        
        **Three ways to handle this:**
        
        1. Present the game plan to them in writing.
        2. Present the overall desire and build the game plan together.
        3. Present the overall desire and have them build the game plan alone and then send to you.
            1. This is my preferred model because them building it locks in more personal responsibility and heart.
            2. It may take you an extra day or two but can be very powerful
        
        *CAUTION: Do not be vague on this stuff!*
        
        - Instead of: “Be more organized.” Say: “Weekly reports submitted by Thursday at noon.”
        - Instead of: “Lead better.” Say: “Two 1:1s with volunteers every month, with written notes.”
        
        Regardless of which way you handle it, we recommend you both sign and date the final plan.
        
        Confirms that they understand where we are going, what the expectations are, and how we’re going to get there. Once they’ve confirmed that, then we will move to the next step.
        
    - **5. CHECK-IN CALENDAR**
        
        **💬** Keep it in front of us
        
        ---
        
        - What is the weekly reporting rhythm?
        - What metrics will be reviewed at each check-in?
        - What format will updates follow?
        - What is the final evaluation date?
        - How will wins and corrections be documented?
        
        The next step is focused on our reporting and check-in schedule.
        
        This is typically determined by their “peel off” rate. How long can they go without peel off focus or getting misaligned?
        
        It can also be set up according to your own needs depending on how you experience a lack of information/updating. Hunter vs Mark.
        
        A recommendation would be to just use the weekly 3 daily 3 model in Middle Method. It makes it simple and keeps things fresh. If you need more information then ask for a daily or weekly write up.
        
        💡*Small things done frequently almost always win the day.*
        
        If the plan is set up to run for the whole cycle then:
        
        - Weekly 3
        - Daily 3
        - REPS
        - Monthly update - face to face for 5-15 minutes
        - Evaluation
    - **6. CLOSE**
        
        **💬** Ask them to summarize the plan verbally in their own words
        
        ---
        
        - What commitments are they making?
        - What does success look like in their words?
        - When will they send written confirmation?
        - Does anything remain unclear before we close?
        
        I would like them to go back and consider our discussion, and then shoot me an email that lays out what they think we are doing and why.
        
        If it is in alignment, then I will confirm it. If it is not, then I will make adjustments and ask them to try again.

    # Tips for you as the leader
    
    - 1. **Frame What a Performance Improvement Plan Is**
        
        Before the meeting, here is the internal posture:
        
        A performance improvement plan is not punishment.
        
        It is a focused intervention. It is simply this:
        
        > "We are not aligned on what winning looks like right now, and we need to reset and see measurable change next cycle."
        >

- **How to Handle Spouse Conversations Without Creating More Hurt** — Navigating the emotional ripple effects when staff transitions impact families and relationships inside the church.
    
    ---
    
    ## Frame the Reality First
    
    Open the toggle with a hard truth:
    
    > *"You're not just transitioning a staff member. You're transitioning a marriage, a family, and a friendship circle inside your church."*
    > 
    
    When a staff member exits, the spouse is processing:
    
    - Loss of income
    - Loss of identity ("I was the pastor's wife / kids' pastor's husband")
    - Loss of community (the church *was* their social world)
    - Loss of trust (in you, the board, the church)
    - Embarrassment, anger, grief, sometimes betrayal
    
    If you don't lead this part well, the spouse becomes the long-tail damage — and they'll often outlast the staff member emotionally in the church's bloodstream.
    
    ---
    
    ## Suggested Structure: The 5 P's of Spouse Conversations
    
    A clean framework that mirrors your other "C" patterns:
    
    ### **1. POSTURE — How you show up matters more than what you say**
    
    - Approach as a pastor, not a manager
    - Lower the room — soft tone, no defensiveness, no HR voice
    - Lead with empathy, not explanation
    - Remember: they were not in the PIP meetings, they only heard *their spouse's* version
    
    ### **2. PERMISSION — Don't initiate without invitation**
    
    - Do **not** call the spouse before the staff member is told
    - Do **not** corner them at church the next Sunday
    - Ask the staff member: *"Would it be helpful for me to talk with [spouse]? If so, when and how would you like that to happen?"*
    - Let the staff member be the one to open or close that door
    
    ### **3. PRESENCE — Show up in the right configuration**
    
    - Don't go alone (especially with opposite-gender spouse) — bring your own spouse, an elder's wife, or another pastor
    - Don't ambush — schedule it, let them prepare emotionally
    - Don't do it on church property unless they request it
    - Keep it short — 30–45 minutes max, not a 3-hour debrief
    
    ### **4. POSTURE OF LANGUAGE — Watch your words**
    
    **Say:**
    
    - "We grieve this with you."
    - "Your worth to us, and to the Kingdom, hasn't changed."
    - "We're not going to speak negatively of [spouse] — and we hope the same in return."
    - "How can we love you well in this transition?"
    
    **Don't say:**
    
    - "We had no choice." *(Defensive.)*
    - "He/she didn't tell you everything." *(Triangulation.)*
    - "God closed the door." *(Spiritual manipulation.)*
    - "We still want you to serve here." *(Almost never realistic.)*
    - "If you ever want to talk about what really happened…" *(Drama bait.)*
    
    ### **5. PATH FORWARD — Give them a runway, not a void**
    
    Offer specific, finite support:
    
    - Paid counseling (3–6 sessions covered)
    - A "soft goodbye" with key friends if they want it
    - A letter from leadership thanking the family (private, not public)
    - Permission to leave the church without guilt — or to stay without weirdness
    - A 90-day check-in from one designated person (so they aren't bombarded by everyone)

- **How to Lead Volunteer Teams Through Staff Transitions -** What to Say to Volunteers When Staffing Changes Happen
    
    Most pastors over-communicate to the staff and under-communicate to the volunteers.
    
    That's where the real damage happens.
    
    Volunteers don't quit because someone left. They quit because nobody talked to them about it.
    
    A staff transition is not a staff event. It's a volunteer event with a staff trigger.
    
    ---
    
    # The 5 V's of Volunteer Communication
    
    - **1. VISIBILITY — Tell them before they hear it**
        
        **💬** No volunteer should learn this in the parking lot.
        
        ---
        
        - The volunteers most loyal to the departing staff member should hear it from you, not from a group text
        - Same week. Often same day.
        - One direct phone call beats ten hallway conversations
        - Order: Board → Staff → Key volunteers who served closest to that staff member → Their full volunteer team → Stop
        
        Most pastors skip step three. That's where the rumor mill starts.
        
    - **2. VOICE — Control the narrative without spinning**
        
        **💬** Give them a clean sentence or they'll invent a messy one.
        
        ---
        
        Hand every volunteer leader one sentence they can repeat when other volunteers ask.
        
        Something like:
        
        > "[Name] is transitioning off staff. We're grateful for their season here. We're focused on what's next for the team."
        > 
        
        That's it.
        
        No backstory. No "what really happened." No code language.
        
        If you give them a clean sentence, they'll repeat it.
        
        If you don't, they'll write one for you.
        
    - **3. VALIDATION — Honor what they built**
        
        **💬** Don't act like the last season didn't happen.
        
        ---
        
        Say it plainly:
        
        - "You built something real under their leadership."
        - "That work matters. It doesn't disappear because they did."
        - "The volunteers they recruited are still our volunteers. The systems they built are still our systems."
        
        Volunteers need permission to grieve without feeling disloyal to the church.
        
        Without that permission, they leave quietly. With it, most of them stay.
        
    - **4. VISION — Give them a why for the future**
        
        **💬** A transition without a vision feels like a funeral.
        
        ---
        
        A transition with a vision feels like a hand-off.
        
        Show them:
        
        - Who is covering responsibilities right now
        - What the next 30, 60, 90 days look like
        - When they'll meet the next leader
        - What is **not** changing (this matters more than what is)
        
        Volunteers will follow a runway. They won't follow a void.
        
    - **5. VICINITY — Stay close through the wobble**
        
        **💬** Presence preaches louder than announcements.
        
        ---
        
        The first 30 days after a staff exit are the most fragile.
        
        - Assign a pastoral point of contact for the volunteer team. Not always you.
        - Check in weekly. Short, specific, human.
        - Show up at one of their gatherings in the first two weeks.
        - Pray for them by name from the stage without naming the situation.
        
        Proximity beats explanation.
        
    
    ---
    
    # What To Say
    
    - "[Name] is transitioning off staff. Today is their last day on the team."
    - "We're grateful for their season here and what they built with you."
    - "Here's who you can talk to in the meantime: [Name, Role, Contact]."
    - "Nothing about your role is changing. We need you now more than ever."
    - "You're free to grieve this. You're free to ask questions. You're not free to spread speculation — and I trust you on that."
    
    # What Not To Say
    
    - "We can't go into the details." *(Sounds like a cover-up.)*
    - "It was mutual." *(Half the time it isn't, and they'll find out.)*
    - "God just released them." *(Spiritual manipulation.)*
    - "Pray for them." *(Without specifics, it sounds like a brush-off.)*
    - "Don't talk about this with anyone." *(Guarantees they will.)*
    - Anything that throws the departing staff under the bus. Ever.
    
    ---
    
    # Special Scenarios
    
    - 🟡 **The Loyal Lieutenant**
        
        The volunteer who was effectively a second-in-command to the departing staff. They feel it the hardest.
        
        - Talk to them first. Privately. Before the rest of the team.
        - Ask them to help carry continuity through the transition.
        - Give them a real role for the next 30 days, not a consolation prize.
        - Don't ask them to defend the decision. Ask them to steward the team.
    - 🟡 **The Angry Volunteer**
        
        The one who shows up loud the next Sunday.
        
        - Don't argue. Don't defend.
        - Listen for the real question underneath the volume — usually fear of change.
        - Offer one private conversation. After that, hold the line.
        - Their anger is rarely about you. Don't make it about you.
    - 🟡 **The Quiet Quitter**
        
        The volunteer who just stops showing up.
        
        - Reach out once. Personally. No pressure.
        - Acknowledge the loss. Don't recruit them back in the same conversation.
        - Give them a soft door to return through in 60 days.
        - Some will come back. Some won't. Both are okay.
    - 🟡 **When the staff member is still in the church**
        - Decide in advance what role they can and cannot play with the volunteer team
        - Communicate that to the volunteers clearly so nobody is guessing
        - Most of the time: a clean break from the volunteer team for 90 days, then revisit
        - The vacuum needs a season of stillness before someone else fills it
    - 🟡 **When the staff member was let go for misconduct**
        - Volunteers deserve enough truth to protect themselves and others
        - Not the gory details. The protective ones.
        - Bring an elder or HR partner into the room when you say it
        - Never improvise this conversation. Write it down first.
        - Err on the side of more direct, not more vague — vagueness in this scenario erodes trust faster than truth does
    - 🟡 **When a whole volunteer team threatens to leave**
        - Expect it. Don't panic.
        - Some will leave. Bless them.
        - The ones who stay are the foundation of the next season.
        - The team rebuilds. It always rebuilds. Faster than you think.
        - Don't chase. Don't beg. Don't bargain.

- **Bonus #3:** **Staff Values Document** - The Culture Guide That Helps Staff Stay Aligned and Accountable
    
    ---
    
    ## Part 1: How to Teach This
    
    ### The Setup — What to Say First
    
    Before you show any examples, land the distinction that unlocks everything.
    
    > *"Responsibilities describe what your team does. Values describe how they do it. Most churches have job descriptions. Almost none of them have team values. That's why feedback gets personal. That's why culture drifts. Without shared values, the standard for behavior is the pastor's mood. With them, you have a language the whole team can use."*
    > 
    
    > *"Values don't just describe your culture. They protect it — especially under pressure, during growth, and in seasons of change. When things get hard, values are what you point to instead of personalities."*
    > 
    
    ---
    
    ### The Three Things Values Give You
    
    Make sure pastors understand why this isn't just a wall poster exercise.
    
    - A shared language for behavior and attitude
    - A filter for hiring, planning, and partnerships
    - A way to give feedback that's constructive — not personal
    
    **The key reframe:** Without values, feedback sounds like "you're not measuring up to me." With values, feedback sounds like "this doesn't match what we've agreed on together." That's a completely different conversation.
    
    ---
    
    ### The Five Criteria — What Makes a Value Actually Work
    
    Walk through each one. This prevents them from writing vague wall-poster platitudes.
    
    **1. Simple enough to remember**
    If it takes a paragraph to explain, it won't guide behavior. It has to be repeatable in a hallway conversation.
    
    **2. Guides real decisions**
    A value should tell someone what to do when they're not sure what to do. "We pursue excellence" tells them nothing. "We finish what we start" tells them something.
    
    **3. Specific to your culture**
    Generic values (integrity, excellence, teamwork) describe every church. Your values should describe yours — and feel slightly wrong for a church that isn't you.
    
    **4. Behavioral, not aspirational**
    Values describe how your team actually behaves — not how you wish they would. Aspirational values are fine to aim for, but they have to be grounded in something real.
    
    **5. Written by the lead pastor**
    Not the team. Not a committee. The lead pastor sets the culture standard. This is one of the most important things you can write on behalf of your team.
    
    ---
    
    ### The Writing Prompt — How to Help Them Get Started
    
    Give them this question to unlock the first draft.
    
    > *"Think about the best season your team has ever had — or imagine the team you're trying to build. What was different about how people showed up? Not what they accomplished. How they worked. Write five words or short phrases that describe that. Don't edit yet. Just write."*
    > 
    
    **Then narrow it:** Once they have their list, have them ask — "Is this specific enough to guide a hard conversation? Or is it so general everyone already agrees with it?" If everyone already agrees, it's not doing any work.
    
    ---
    
    ### The Format — What Each Value Needs
    
    Every value should include three things:
    
    - **A name** — short, memorable, specific
    - **What it looks like** — two or three concrete behaviors
    - **What it doesn't look like** — the most common failure mode
    
    > *"The 'what it doesn't look like' section is the one most pastors skip. Don't skip it. That's the section that actually makes values usable. It's where the real culture standard lives."*
    > 
    
    ---
    
    ---
    
    ## Part 2: Sample Values Document
    
    *Use this as a model. Adapt it to fit your church.*
    
    ---
    
    **Responsibilities describe what we do. Values describe how we do it.**
    
    These five characteristics define how we work together to accomplish the vision. They are filters for planning, hiring, decisions, and feedback.
    
    ---
    
    ### Value 01 — We Own Outcomes, Not Efforts
    
    *Results over activity*
    
    We take full responsibility for what gets accomplished — not just what we attempted. When obstacles appear, we find solutions. We follow through until value is delivered, not just until work is "done."
    
    **What this looks like:**
    
    - Taking responsibility for results, not just activities
    - Finding a way when the original plan doesn't work
    - Following through until it's actually finished
    
    **What this doesn't look like:**
    
    - Saying "I did my part" when the outcome failed
    - Working hard without asking if the work matters
    - Reporting problems without bringing solutions
    
    ---
    
    ### Value 02 — We Fight for Clarity
    
    *Specific over assumed*
    
    We ask for specifics instead of assuming. We confirm expectations before starting. We don't operate on vague direction, and we don't give it. Clarity is an act of respect for everyone's time.
    
    **What this looks like:**
    
    - Asking clarifying questions before moving
    - Confirming understanding before starting
    - Being specific in our own communication
    
    **What this doesn't look like:**
    
    - Guessing and hoping it works out
    - Avoiding follow-up questions to seem competent
    - Using vague language to avoid hard conversations
    
    ---
    
    ### Value 03 — We Finish What We Start
    
    *Follow-through over intentions*
    
    We see initiatives through to real completion — not just initial launch. We address the loose ends. We don't declare victory early, and we don't move to the next thing while the current thing is still unresolved.
    
    **What this looks like:**
    
    - Delivering on commitments consistently
    - Addressing the details, not just the big moves
    - Seeing it through to actual completion
    
    **What this doesn't look like:**
    
    - Starting many things and completing few
    - Declaring something "done" when it's just launched
    - Moving on before the loose ends are resolved
    
    ---
    
    ### Value 04 — We Debate, Then Commit
    
    *Honest in the room, aligned after*
    
    We argue passionately for different viewpoints while we're deciding. Once a decision is made, we support it fully — even if we initially disagreed. Disagreement in the room is healthy. Undermining after the room is a culture killer.
    
    **What this looks like:**
    
    - Voicing disagreement openly and respectfully
    - Fully supporting decisions once they're made
    - Bringing concerns to the table, not the hallway
    
    **What this doesn't look like:**
    
    - Agreeing in the room, criticizing afterward
    - Avoiding conflict to maintain false peace
    - Relitigating closed decisions with the wrong people
    
    ---
    
    ### Value 05 — We Pick on Systems, Not on People
    
    *Fix the process, not the person*
    
    When something breaks, we ask what process led to that result — not who failed. We identify system flaws, suggest improvements, and create structures that help everyone. Blame is a dead end. Better systems are not.
    
    **What this looks like:**
    
    - Asking "what broke?" before "who messed up?"
    - Suggesting improvements that help the whole team
    - Taking personal responsibility while fixing the system
    
    **What this doesn't look like:**
    
    - Blaming individuals without examining the system
    - Personalizing failure instead of improving processes
    - Using mistakes as an opportunity to point fingers
    
    ---

Change the names. Rewrite the descriptions. Make them yours. The format is the gift. The content has to come from you.
