# A-Team Workshop — Framework Visuals Brief

*Designed to be handed to a visual AI tool (Canva, Midjourney, Figma AI, ChatGPT image gen, etc.). Each framework includes the core logic, component breakdown, the visual concept that best represents it, and the exact labels to use.*

---

# FRAMEWORK 1 — THE TWO DOORS

*The meta-framework. This is the overarching visual for the workshop.*

## The Logic

Every team has two doors: a Front Door (hiring) and a Back Door (firing). Most pastors operate both doors on emotion. The fix is installing a written discipline at each door. When both doors are closed clean, the team becomes an A-Team. When either door leaks, the team degrades.

**The relationship is symmetrical.** Front Door and Back Door are mirror images of the same discipline: clarity in writing before emotion enters the room.

## Visual Concept

**Style:** A simple architectural diagram of a building or stadium with two clearly labeled doors. Or — even cleaner — two literal doorways side by side with a team or arrow flowing between them.

**Alt visual:** A horizontal flow with FRONT DOOR on the left, the TEAM in the center, BACK DOOR on the right.

## Components & Labels

```
                    THE A-TEAM
            (the team that gets installed)

  [FRONT DOOR]                          [BACK DOOR]
  Hire for the Five                     The 7 C's
  + The 90+ Rule                        of Separation

  Discipline:                           Discipline:
  Score before you offer.               Decide before you deliver.

  Failure mode:                         Failure mode:
  Hired on a feeling.                   Fired on guilt.
```

## Visual Emphasis Notes

- Both doors should look equally important. Not a big door and a little door.
- Show the *team* in the middle as the outcome — not the doors themselves.
- Tagline at the bottom: ***Win the doors. Win the team.***

# FRAMEWORK 2 — HIRE FOR THE FIVE

*The five non-negotiables you score every candidate against.*

## The Logic

Most pastors hire on gut and gifting. The 5 C's force you to score five filters before extending an offer. Each C catches a specific failure mode. They are not weighted equally — Character and Culture are non-negotiables. Skill (Competency) gets you in the door but doesn't keep you in the role.

**The hierarchy matters.** Character + Culture are the floor. Competency + Capacity are the ceiling. Chemistry is the connective tissue. The visual should reflect that some C's are foundational and others are conditional.

## Visual Concept

**Style:** Five clean blocks or pillars, each labeled with one C and one diagnostic question. Could be:
- Five vertical pillars supporting a single beam labeled "The Hire"
- A pentagon with one C at each point
- A pyramid where Character/Culture form the base, Capacity/Competency the middle, Chemistry the peak

**Recommended:** Five pillars. Easiest to read. Easiest to remember.

## Components & Labels

| C | One-Line Definition | What It Catches |
|---|---|---|
| **Competency** | They have *demonstrated* the skill. | Hiring on potential without proof. |
| **Character** | Their private life matches their public life. | Strong testimony hiding weak character. |
| **Chemistry** | The team gets better when they walk in. | Pastor-only chemistry checks. |
| **Capacity** | They can grow into the *next* version of this role. | Hiring people maxed out on day one. |
| **Culture** | They already live your values. | Hiring "good people" who need to be rewired. |

## Visual Emphasis Notes

- **Character and Culture should be visually heavier** than the other three (bigger font, bolder color, or labeled "×2").
- Each pillar gets a one-line definition under the letter.
- The roof or top label: ***The Five Non-Negotiables.***
- Punch close beneath the diagram: ***Hire the person you already know you have.***

# FRAMEWORK 3 — THE 90+ RULE

*The hiring scorecard that forces a number before you make the offer.*

## The Logic

Once you have the 5 C's, you score them. Plus five behavioral C's that round out the picture. Ten total scores, 1–10 each. You're hunting for 90 or above. But the total isn't the only rule — there are two disciplines that override the total.

**Three rules layered on top of the score:**
1. **The Floor:** Any single score under 7 = automatic no. Even if total is 95.
2. **The Weight:** Character and Culture count double. They are not equal to the others.
3. **The Pen:** Every interviewer scores their own card. In writing. No verbal scoring.

**Color bands matter:**
- 90–100 = Green (Hire)
- 75–89 = Yellow (Probably no)
- Under 75 = Red (Hard no)

## Visual Concept

**Style:** A vertical scorecard with the 10 C's listed, each with a 1–10 slider or score box. Character and Culture are visually marked with a "×2" or doubled box. At the bottom, a total bar with three color bands.

**Alt visual:** A "thermometer" style graphic — the total filling up vertically through red → yellow → green zones, with the 90 line clearly marked.

**Alt visual:** A scorecard mock-up that looks like an actual interview clipboard.

## Components & Labels

```
THE 90+ RULE — Hiring Scorecard

  1.  Competency        ___/10
  2.  Character (×2)    ___/10  ← non-negotiable
  3.  Chemistry         ___/10
  4.  Capacity          ___/10
  5.  Culture (×2)      ___/10  ← non-negotiable
  6.  Calling           ___/10
  7.  Coachability      ___/10
  8.  Conviction        ___/10
  9.  Consistency       ___/10
  10. Confidence        ___/10

  TOTAL:    ___/100

  ┌──────────────────────────┐
  │ 90–100  GREEN  → HIRE    │
  │ 75–89   YELLOW → HOLD    │
  │ <75     RED    → NO      │
  └──────────────────────────┘

  RULES:
  • Any single score below 7 = automatic NO
  • Character + Culture weighted ×2
  • Every interviewer fills their own card
```

## Visual Emphasis Notes

- The **90 line** should be the most visually prominent element. A bright line at 90.
- Character and Culture rows should stand out (highlight, bold, or "×2" tag).
- Three color bands — red, yellow, green — non-negotiable visual element.
- Tagline under the scorecard: ***Score it. Or you'll suffer it.***

# FRAMEWORK 4 — THE 7 C'S OF A HEALTHY SEPARATION

*The seven-step discipline for closing a season cleanly.*

## The Logic

A firing is an event. A separation is a process. The 7 C's walk a leader from internal preparation through the conversation, the practicals, the family, and the final close. The order is non-negotiable. Skipping a step is how exits go sideways.

**This is a sequence, not a menu.** You don't get to pick your favorite three. Each step builds on the last.

## Visual Concept

**Style:** A horizontal seven-step process flow, like a journey map or a staircase. Each step labeled with the C, a one-sentence summary, and an icon.

**Alt visual:** A circular flow (since this is a cycle a leader may need to walk multiple times in their tenure). The center reads ***Grief without chaos. Honor without spectacle.***

**Alt visual:** A vertical flow that reads like a checklist a leader can run through pre-meeting.

## Components & Labels

| # | Step | One-Line Summary |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Center** | Am I emotionally ready to lead this well? |
| 2 | **Confirm** | The decision is made *before* the meeting. Not in it. |
| 3 | **Communicate** | Don't bury the lead. Three sentences. Then stop. |
| 4 | **Care (Honor)** | Affirm the person without reversing the decision. |
| 5 | **Calm Clarity (HR)** | Pay, severance, equipment, comms — prepared in advance. |
| 6 | **Care for Family** | Acknowledge the family without over-pastoring. |
| 7 | **Close** | End with finality. No false hope. No open doors. |

## Visual Emphasis Notes

- Each step should be **clearly numbered** — the sequence matters.
- Use a consistent icon set (one per step) to make it visually memorable.
- The arc should feel **calm and dignified**, not corporate or punitive.
- Tagline below the diagram: ***Clarity is kinder than confusion.***

# FRAMEWORK 5 — THE ORDER OF COMMUNICATION

*Who hears what, when, and from whom — after the decision.*

## The Logic

Most staff exits don't fall apart in the meeting. They fall apart in the *announcement.* When communication runs ahead of leadership, the rumor mill wins. The order protects everyone — the departing staff member, the team, the volunteers, the church.

**The order is one-directional. You cannot reverse it.** Board first. Always. Stop at the team. Almost never go to the whole congregation.

## Visual Concept

**Style:** A linear arrow chain, left to right. Each box is a recipient, with a brief note on *what* they hear and *when.* The final box is a hard STOP sign.

**Alt visual:** Concentric circles — Board at the center, Staff next ring, Volunteers next ring, Team next ring, and a clear **STOP** boundary before the congregation ring.

**Recommended:** The arrow chain. It reads as a *sequence*, which is what this is.

## Components & Labels

```
1. BOARD          →  2. STAFF MEMBER  →  3. KEY VOLUNTEERS  →  4. THEIR TEAM  →  ❌ STOP

(before anything    (immediately after    (same day, direct      (walk through
 — get alignment,    the conversation —    phone call — they      what's changing,
 approval, prayer    no secondhand          deserve direct,        who covers what,
 cover)              learning)              not hallway)           when)


DO NOT:
• Announce to the whole church
• Throw a public "thank you" party
• Make it a stage moment
• Post a tribute on social media
```

## Visual Emphasis Notes

- The **STOP** at the end is as important as any of the steps. Make it loud — red box, big X, hard line.
- Number each step. The sequence is the whole point.
- Tagline beneath: ***Grief without chaos. Honor without spectacle.***

# BONUS — THE LIVE APPLICATION WORKSHEET

*Not a framework, but the worksheet pastors fill in during the workshop. Worth a visual.*

## The Logic

The workshop ends with two concrete acts: scoring one real hire and drafting the opening of one avoided conversation. The worksheet should fit on a single page so pastors leave with something tangible.

## Visual Concept

**Style:** A clean one-pager, two-column or top-half/bottom-half. Top: the 90+ Rule scorecard for a current hire. Bottom: the three-sentence draft for the back-door conversation.

## Components & Labels

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  STEP 1 — SCORE A REAL HIRE                     │
│  Name: _______________                          │
│  [Insert the 10-C scorecard from Framework 3]   │
│  What did this just tell you that you           │
│  didn't want to admit? _______________          │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  STEP 2 — DRAFT THE OPENING                     │
│  The person: _______________                    │
│  Sentence 1 (Set the tone): ______________      │
│  Sentence 2 (Name reality):  ______________      │
│  Sentence 3 (Deliver next):  ______________      │
│                                                 │
│  STEP 3 — SET THE DATE                          │
│  I will say these words on: ___ / ___ / ___    │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Visual Emphasis Notes

- **The date line is the most important field on the page.** Make it visually heavy.
- Worksheet should fit on one page, portrait or landscape.
- Bottom of the worksheet: ***Win the doors. Win the team.***

# OVERALL VISUAL STYLE NOTES (for the AI tool)

- **Style:** Clean, modern, sans-serif. Think Basecamp or Notion — not corporate consulting.
- **Color palette:** Restrained — one accent color (deep blue or forest green works well), black/white/grey for the rest. Red and green only where they carry meaning (scorecard bands, STOP signs).
- **Tone:** Calm, dignified, structured. Not loud, not pastel, not "leadership-bro."
- **Type hierarchy:** Big framework name. Smaller component labels. Tiny supporting text. Always a punch close at the bottom.
- **Repeating element:** Every framework should end with its **punch close** as a final line beneath the diagram. This is the RSG signature.
