# Email formats template

Use this template when drafting any email. It covers the main types you'll write — replies, cold outreach, follow-ups, and announcements. Tell Claude which type you need, or just describe what you want and it will pick the right format.

---

## What Claude needs from you

**The context:** Who are you writing to? What's the relationship? (New contact, existing client, colleague, cold outreach.) If replying, paste the original email or tell Claude to check your Gmail.

**What you want to say:** A few bullet points or a sentence. What's the core message?

**The ask:** What do you want the recipient to do after reading? Reply, book a call, approve something, pay an invoice, click a link? Be specific.

**Tone override:** If this needs a different tone from your default (set in your About Me files), say so. "More formal than usual" or "Keep it warm, this is a friend."

---

## Format: Reply

**When:** Responding to an email you've received.

**Structure:**
1. Acknowledge naturally — no "Thank you for reaching out" or "I hope this finds you well." Get into it.
2. Lead with the answer or the main thing they need to know. Don't bury it.
3. Supporting detail if needed — context, reasoning, specifics. Keep it tight.
4. Clear next step. "I'll send the draft by Friday" beats "Let's stay in touch."
5. Close that matches the relationship. "Cheers," "Thanks," "Speak soon" — not corporate unless the situation calls for it.

---

## Format: Introduction or cold outreach

**When:** Reaching out to someone who doesn't know you (or doesn't know you well).

**Structure:**
1. One sentence on who you are and why you're writing. No life story.
2. The reason this is relevant to them specifically — not a generic pitch. What have you noticed about their work, their business, their situation?
3. What you're proposing — clear, specific, low-commitment. A 15-minute call, a quick look at something, a specific collaboration idea.
4. Easy out. "No worries if the timing isn't right" — gives them permission to say no without awkwardness.
5. Sign off with your name and one relevant link (website, LinkedIn, portfolio — not all three).

---

## Format: Follow-up

**When:** You've emailed before and haven't heard back.

**Structure:**
1. Quick reference to your previous email — "I sent a note last week about X."
2. Add something new. Don't just repeat yourself. A new angle, a relevant update, a question that's easier to answer than the original ask.
3. Make responding easy. Yes/no question, or two clear options.
4. Keep it shorter than the original. Follow-ups should be lighter, not heavier.

---

## Format: Announcement or update

**When:** Sharing news, launching something, or updating a group.

**Structure:**
1. Lead with the news. First sentence tells them what happened or what's changing.
2. Why it matters to them. Not why it matters to you — why should they care?
3. What they need to do (if anything). If no action needed, say so: "Nothing you need to do — just wanted you to know."
4. Where to learn more or ask questions.

## Good email vs bad email

**Bad:** "I wanted to reach out to introduce myself and explore potential synergies between our respective organisations. Having followed your work for some time, I believe there could be mutually beneficial opportunities for collaboration. Would you be open to scheduling a call at your earliest convenience to discuss this further?"

**Good:** "Hi — I'm [name], I run [company]. I saw your talk at [event] and loved your point about [specific thing]. I've been working on something similar and think there might be a natural collaboration. Would you be up for a 15-minute call next week? Happy to work around your schedule."

The good version is specific, personal, and makes it easy to say yes.
