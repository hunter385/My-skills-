# Client proposal / brief template

Use this template when writing a proposal for a client, a project pitch, a partnership brief, or any document where you're presenting what you'll do, how you'll do it, and what it costs. Adapt the sections to fit your context — not every project needs every section.

---

## The proposal

### Project title

[Clear, descriptive name. "Website Redesign for Acme Co" not "Project Proposal".]

### Prepared for

[Client name, company, date]

### The problem

[What problem are you solving? Why does this matter to the client? Write this from their perspective, not yours. What's the pain they're feeling or the opportunity they're missing?]

### What we'll do

[Your proposed solution in plain language. Not a list of deliverables yet — the story of what you'll build, create, or fix, and why this approach is the right one.]

### Deliverables

[Now the specifics. What exactly will the client receive? Be precise.]

- [Deliverable 1 — with enough detail that there's no ambiguity]
- [Deliverable 2]
- [Deliverable 3]

### Timeline

[When does work start? Key milestones? When is it done?]

| Phase | What happens | Duration |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| [Phase 1] | [Description] | [X weeks] |
| [Phase 2] | [Description] | [X weeks] |
| [Delivery] | [Final handover] | [Date] |

### Investment

[What it costs. Be clear and confident — no apologetic language around pricing.]

| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| [Service/deliverable] | [Amount] |
| [Service/deliverable] | [Amount] |
| **Total** | **[Amount]** |

**Payment terms:** [When payment is due. Deposit? Milestone-based? On completion?]

### Why us

[Brief section on why you're the right fit. Not a generic "about us" — specific reasons this client should work with you on this project. Previous relevant work, specific expertise, testimonials if you have them.]

### Next steps

[What happens if they say yes. Make it easy. "Reply to this email and we'll schedule a kick-off call for next week."]

## How to use this

**Option 1: Fill in the template yourself.** Complete the sections, then ask Claude to polish: "Here's my proposal draft. Tighten the language and make it persuasive without being salesy."

**Option 2: Co-create with Claude.** Tell Claude about the project and client, then ask it to draft the proposal: "I need a proposal for [client] for [project]. Here's what I know: [details]."

**Option 3: Start from a conversation.** If you've been emailing with the client, paste the relevant emails and ask Claude to draft a proposal based on what's been discussed.

---

## Notes

- The "problem" section is the most important part. If the client doesn't recognise their problem in your words, they won't read the rest.
- Keep it short. Most proposals are too long. One page is better than five if it covers everything.
- Price confidently. No "we'd suggest an investment of" — just state the number.
- For a branded version, tell Claude to create it as a Word document or PDF using your brand style.
