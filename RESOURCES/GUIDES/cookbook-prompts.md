## The 5 principles

### 1. Describe the destination, not the journey

CoWork is an agent, not a chatbot. Tell it what "done" looks like — describe outcomes, not steps.

Instead of "Can you help me organise my files?" try:

> Organise my Downloads folder by type. Create subfolders for Documents, Images, Spreadsheets, and Other. Rename each file with a YYYY-MM-DD prefix based on modification date. Create a summary log of all changes.


### 2. Front-load what Claude can't guess

Your About Me files handle the persistent stuff — who you are, how you write, where your tools live. But each prompt still needs the context that changes every time: what does "done" look like, what can't Claude infer, and what constraints apply?

Instead of "Write me a reply to this email" try:

> Draft a reply to this email from a potential sponsor. Context: they're offering a 3-video deal at $5K per video. I like the product but the rate is low — I typically charge $8-12K. Tone: warm but confident. Goal: counter-offer without burning the relationship.


### 3. Push back and iterate

First drafts are starting points. The best users treat Claude like a capable colleague whose work needs direction — not a vending machine that either works or doesn't.

Useful iteration moves:

- "This is too generic. Make it more specific to [my situation]."
- "Good structure, wrong tone. Make it sound more like me."
- "What did you leave out? What's the strongest counterargument?"
- "Cut this in half. Every sentence needs to earn its place."
- "You're being too safe. Give me the version you'd write if you weren't trying to be balanced."


### 4. Make Claude think harder

When a task is complex, vague, or high-stakes, don't just ask for the answer — ask Claude to earn it.

**Show Your Working:** "Walk me through your reasoning step by step. After each step, ask yourself: could this be wrong? If yes, say why."
Use when: making decisions, evaluating options, assessing risks.

**Unpack My Question:** "Before answering, list every implicit assumption baked into my question. Then answer."
Use when: planning something new, writing a brief, scoping a project.

**Remove the Easy Answer:** "Solve this with the constraint that you can't use the obvious solution. What's the second-best approach?"
Use when: you're stuck, the obvious path is blocked, or you want creative alternatives.

**The Sceptic's Chair:** "Explain this as if the most sceptical person in the room is trying to poke holes in it. What would they say?"
Use when: reviewing a plan, checking a draft, pressure-testing an idea.

**Confidence Check:** "Complete this task, then list every assumption you made that could be wrong. Rate each 1-10 on confidence."
Use when: any high-stakes output — contracts, strategy docs, public content.

*Thinking moves inspired by Ruben Hassid's "7 Prompts That Make AI Think Harder"*


### 5. Build systems, not one-offs

The most powerful use of CoWork isn't the individual prompt — it's the reusable workflow. Templates, scheduled tasks, and specialist plugins turn one-off tasks into repeatable systems.

- "We just did that well. Save the approach as a template I can reuse."
- "I do this every week. Can we turn this into a scheduled task?"
- "I keep asking you for the same kind of help with [domain]. Should we build a specialist for this?"

## 15 Quick Wins

### Tier 1: Instant gratification
Works in the first 10 minutes — just needs your About Me files filled in.

**1. Voice test**
> Write a short LinkedIn post about [topic you know well]. Use my voice profile and writing rules.

**2. Rewrite in my voice**
> Rewrite this in my voice: [paste any AI-generated or corporate text]

**3. Research and summarise**
> Research [topic] and give me a structured summary: what it is, why it matters for my work, the three most important things to know, and what I should do next.

**4. Quick decision**
> I need to decide between [option A] and [option B]. Before advising, list the assumptions in my question. Then evaluate both against what you know about me and my business from my About Me files.

**5. Email draft**
> Draft a reply to this email: [paste email]. Keep it warm and direct, match my voice, and keep it under 100 words.


### Tier 2: Connectors alive
Needs Gmail, Calendar, or Notion connected.

**6. Inbox triage**
> Check my Gmail from the last 3 days. Categorise emails into: needs reply, FYI only, and can ignore. For the "needs reply" ones, draft a short reply for each — save as drafts, don't send.

**7. Calendar briefing**
> What's on my calendar this week? For each meeting, tell me who's attending, what it's likely about, and whether I need to prepare anything.

**8. Notion task check**
> Check my Notion task database. What's overdue? What's due this week? Anything that's been sitting for more than 2 weeks without progress?

**9. Meeting prep**
> I have a meeting with [person/company] tomorrow. Search my email and Notion for any previous correspondence or notes. Summarise what I know about them and suggest three things to bring up.

**10. Content pipeline check**
> Check my content database in Notion. What's in progress? What's overdue? What should I focus on next?


### Tier 3: System power
Demonstrates the full OS — specialists, file creation, scheduled tasks.

**11. Specialist domain**
> I'm planning a new [project type] about [topic]. Help me develop the concept — what's the angle, what are the key points, and what makes this worth doing?

**12. Repurpose content**
> Here's my latest [content piece]: [paste or point to file]. Create versions for Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Instagram, and a community post.

**13. Create a project**
> I'm starting a new project: [brief description]. Set up a project folder with a brief and memory log.

**14. File creation**
> Write a one-page report on [topic] as a Word document. Professional but conversational. Save it to my outputs folder.

**15. Build a scheduled task**
> I want a Monday morning briefing that checks my calendar for the week, my overdue tasks in Notion, and any unread important emails. Set it up as a scheduled task.

## How to use this reference

Start with Tier 1 to prove your system works. Move to Tier 2 once you've connected your tools. Tier 3 shows what's possible once you're comfortable.

The principles are the lasting value — they change how you think about working with AI. The prompts are starting points you'll adapt to your own work.
